Four-door convertibles basically don't exist anymore, which is wild when you realize they used to be completely normal. From the 1920s through the 1960s, makers like Chrysler, Mercedes, and Rolls-Royce built them regularly, treating an open roof and a back seat as no contradiction at all. They weren't some niche oddity either. They were how rich people actually drove, especially in Europe where a summer jaunt meant sun, wind, and passengers who could actually exit without tumbling over the front seat.

The thing that kills them today is the modern rollover safety standard. Convertibles are already structurally softer than hardtops. Add four full doors and the engineering gets legitimately complicated. You need side-impact protection that doesn't exist in a body with no roof, so the industry just stopped trying. It's safer, sure, but it erased an entire category of car that had real character. A Rolls-Royce Phantom drophead from 1930 or a Mercedes-Benz 770 pulled open in traffic and people stared. A 2024 luxury convertible is a two-seater that costs the same.

Jalopnik dug into eight factory builds that actually happened, and the list hits different notes. You've got the stately stuff, cars that existed to project wealth and taste. Then there's the quirky end of the spectrum. A four-door convertible isn't a car you buy because it makes sense. It's a car you buy because you want everyone to know you're not thinking about normal car logic. That's probably the whole point of any of them. They existed in a time when a convertible was just what you chose if you wanted fresh air, and four doors was what you chose if you had a family or wanted to show up with friends. Combine them and you get something that shouldn't work but somehow did.
