Imagine sipping a rich, full-bodied espresso without the need for scalding hot water. That’s exactly what researchers at the University of New South Wales have achieved with their ultrasonic espresso process. Using high-frequency sound waves, they’ve managed to brew a coffee that rivals the conventional method, but with a significant twist: it requires 75 percent less energy.

Francisco Trujillo, a Colombian researcher leading the team, has developed a method that uses ultrasonic waves to extract the flavor, oils, and caffeine from ground coffee. The process, dubbed "ultrasonic espresso," operates at room temperature, eliminating the need for heated water. While it takes longer, about three minutes compared to the 30 seconds of a traditional espresso, it offers a substantial energy-saving advantage.

The key to this innovative brewing technique lies in a phenomenon called acoustic cavitation. Ultrasonic waves create tiny bubbles that collapse, generating microcurrents that facilitate the extraction of soluble compounds from the coffee. The researchers designed a device that transmits these ultrasonic vibrations throughout the filter basket, effectively turning it into an acoustic reactor.

To achieve the desired concentration, the team adjusted variables like grind size, ultrasound power, and brewing time. Using a fine grind and 100 watts of power, they produced beverages with dissolved solids and extraction yields comparable to those of conventional espresso. Sensory tests involving 100 participants showed no notable preference between ultrasonic espresso and traditional espresso, with similar scores for aroma, flavor, and overall acceptance.

The environmental benefits are significant. The ultrasonic system uses just 24 percent of the energy consumed by a typical espresso machine. This could revolutionize the coffee industry, especially for businesses that produce large quantities of coffee, like ready-to-drink products.

While ultrasonic espresso isn’t identical to traditional espresso, it offers a compelling alternative with comparable sensory and chemical characteristics. The research opens up possibilities for developing new coffee makers that can prepare everything from espresso to filtered coffee and cold brew using the same technology. If these systems ever hit the market, the familiar sound of an espresso machine might be replaced by the inaudible vibrations of ultrasound.

This groundbreaking method could be a game-changer for coffee enthusiasts and businesses alike, offering a more energy-efficient way to enjoy a perfect cup of coffee.
