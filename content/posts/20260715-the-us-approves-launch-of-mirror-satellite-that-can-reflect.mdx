The US Federal Communications Commission has just given the green light to Reflect Orbital to launch Eärendil-1, a mirror satellite that can reflect sunlight and illuminate Earth at night. The 18-meter reflector can light up areas up to 6 kilometers in diameter, and it's just the beginning of a potentially massive constellation of 50,000 satellites by 2035. 

Reflect Orbital claims this tech could revolutionize solar energy, rescue operations, and construction sites, but the astronomical community is not thrilled. The European Southern Observatory calls it an "existential threat" to optical astronomy, and the FCC received nearly 2,000 public comments questioning the proposal. Critics worry about increased light pollution, interference with nocturnal ecosystems, and potential risks to aviation safety.

Despite these concerns, the FCC sees Eärendil-1 as a limited tech demo, and any future expansion would require new approvals. Reflect Orbital promises to work with stakeholders and measure real-world impacts, but the idea of the sky filled with moons is a bit too sci-fi for some. Javier Carbajal's image captures the audacity of this project, and it's clear that the night sky as we know it might never be the same.
