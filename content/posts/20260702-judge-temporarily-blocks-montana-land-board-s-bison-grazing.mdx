A Montana judge just handed American Prairie a temporary win in its fight over bison grazing on state trust lands. On June 26, Lewis and Clark County District Court Judge Christopher Abbott granted a preliminary injunction, blocking the Montana Board of Land Commissioners from freezing new bison grazing requests. This isn't just a legal victory; it's a significant step in the ongoing battle between conservation efforts and traditional agriculture.

American Prairie, a conservation nonprofit, has been pushing to restore grassland habitat in north-central Montana, managing a bison herd that grazes on both private and public land. Ranchers and some state officials have long opposed this, arguing that bison grazing interferes with cattle operations and traditional farming. The state's move to freeze bison grazing requests was seen as a direct shot at American Prairie, but the judge's ruling suggests the Land Board may have overstepped its bounds by creating a new rule without following Montana’s formal rulemaking process.

This temporary injunction doesn't settle the broader dispute over bison grazing in Montana, but it does keep the door open for American Prairie to continue its conservation efforts. The DNRC has also proposed to process American Prairie’s request to change the class of livestock on five state leases, which could further complicate the already tangled legal landscape. 

The fight isn't over, but for now, American Prairie can keep pushing its bison grazing requests, ensuring that the conservation battle continues to play out in the courts and on the land.
