Before crossovers became the homogenized boxes clogging every suburban driveway, automakers actually took risks with SUV design. Michael Sullivan pulled together eight vehicles that prove the category used to be genuinely weird, and it's worth remembering what we've traded for efficiency and dealer lots full of gray Teslas.

The piece lands hard on the simple truth: SUVs didn't always look like they came off the same CAD program. You had the Nissan Juke arriving as this wedge-shaped, almost aggressive little thing that made nobody happy but everyone noticed. There's the Chevy HHR, which threw retro styling at the problem and built something genuinely strange. The Pontiac Aztek sits here too, that infamous crossover that tried so hard to be futuristic it became a punchline. Then there's stuff like the Suzuki X-Lander concept, the Isuzu VehiCROSS, and vehicles that took genuine design chances instead of playing it safe.

What matters about this list isn't just that these cars look odd now. It's that they came from an era when SUV buyers might actually want something different from their neighbors, when automakers hadn't figured out there was more money in making everything beige and predictable. The industry converged on one formula because it works, sure. But somewhere between maximizing interior volume and hitting crossover profitability targets, we lost the stuff that made vehicles memorable. Sullivan's roundup is basically a museum of the moment before that happened.
