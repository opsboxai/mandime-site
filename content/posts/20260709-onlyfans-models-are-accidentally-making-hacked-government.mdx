Here’s a wild one: OnlyFans models are inadvertently helping to clean up hacked government websites. Scammers have been exploiting insecure .gov and .edu domains to host malicious content, often disguised as “leaked” OnlyFans material. These compromised sites can appear high in Google search results, luring unsuspecting users into scams or malware traps.

But here’s where it gets interesting: adult content creators, in their relentless pursuit of protecting their intellectual property, have been filing DMCA takedown requests. These requests have led to the removal of thousands of malicious pages from search results, effectively helping to clean up the internet. According to a recent analysis by cybersecurity firm UpGuard, over 2,000 government and educational websites across 80 countries have received these takedown requests, with many of them being repeatedly compromised since 2020.

Laura Lux, an OnlyFans model with nearly two decades of experience in the adult content industry, says, “We do lose a lot of money just because the content is literally a Google search away a lot of the time.” The DMCA takedown process has become a crucial tool for these creators, but it’s also having an unintended side effect: helping to protect the public from malicious content hosted on compromised government and educational sites.

While the DMCA takedown process is effective, it’s not a perfect solution. Dan Purcell, the founder of Ceartas, a firm that helps creators remove pirated content, notes that sending DMCA requests to compromised sites is excessive and inappropriate. He argues that these sites are not intentionally hosting the content and that other methods should be used to clean up hacked official sites.

In the end, it’s a win for both the creators and the public. The DMCA takedown process, while primarily designed to protect intellectual property, is also serving as an unexpected line of defense against internet scams and malware.
