Slate's new electric truck is officially the cheapest EV on the market, coming in at just under $25,000 for the base model. But here's the kicker: it's powered by lithium iron phosphate (LFP) batteries, a technology that's been perfected in China. 

This isn't just a quirky detail; it's a testament to how global supply chains and policy changes can have unexpected consequences. LFP batteries were originally developed in the US but were largely abandoned by Western manufacturers in favor of more energy-dense options. Meanwhile, Chinese companies like BYD and CATL have dominated LFP production, with 97.8% of LFP cathode production now happening in China.

The shift to LFP batteries in the US is partly thanks to President Trump's repeal of EV tax credits that required materials to be sourced domestically. This move made it easier for companies like Slate to use cheaper, Chinese-made LFP batteries without worrying about losing tax incentives. 

While this might seem like a win for affordability, it also highlights the complex interplay between geopolitics, technology, and market forces. As the US market struggles with a confusing and sluggish EV landscape, automakers are increasingly turning to LFPs for more affordable trims. But with China still dominating the supply chain, it's clear that the path to affordable EVs isn't just about innovation, it's also about navigating a global game of supply and demand.
