Imagine a world where the DNA of endangered species is stored in a high-tech vault, ready for future conservation efforts. That's exactly what the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service and Colossal Biosciences are working on. The partnership aims to preserve genetic material from roughly 2,300 endangered and threatened plants and wildlife, a project Colossal is calling the BioVault.

Colossal Biosciences, known for its ambitious de-extinction projects, is no stranger to making headlines. The company recently created genetically edited gray wolves with dire wolf traits, a move that put them in the spotlight. Now, they're teaming up with federal wildlife officials to store and preserve genetic material, potentially saving species from extinction.

The BioVault will house living cells, reproductive tissues, and genomic material, all stored cryogenically in liquid nitrogen at -321 degrees Fahrenheit. Colossal plans to spend tens of millions of dollars to build and operate the project, with no immediate federal funding involved. The goal is to save genetic material before species lose more diversity or disappear entirely.

While the BioVault could provide researchers with more options for assisted reproduction and genetic management, it's important to note that it won't solve the broader issues facing endangered species. Habitat loss, invasive species, and disease are still major threats. The frozen cells won't protect migration corridors, restore wetlands, or keep sagebrush intact.

The announcement comes as the Trump administration is proposing changes to the Endangered Species Act regulations. These changes could affect how threatened species are protected, potentially leaving them with less automatic coverage. The BioVault, therefore, becomes a critical backup plan, ensuring that genetic material is preserved for future recovery efforts.

In essence, the BioVault is a high-tech insurance policy for endangered species. While it's not a silver bullet, it could provide researchers with invaluable resources to help recover species that are on the brink of extinction.
