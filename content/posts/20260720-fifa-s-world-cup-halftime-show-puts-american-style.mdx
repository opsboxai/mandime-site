FIFA just handed a halftime show budget big enough to rent a small country and stuffed it with Shakira, Justin Bieber, Coldplay, and BTS. The gamble is massive: they want the most-watched halftime spectacle in history, which means they're betting that soccer fans tuning in for 90 minutes of football actually want a 20-minute pop concert interrupting their match.

Here's the tension nobody's talking about. The Super Bowl halftime show works because American football has natural dead air. Fans expect a break. They want entertainment, endorsements, and camera cuts. Soccer is continuous. It has rhythm. You stop it for a production number, and you break the spell that's kept people locked in since kickoff. The World Cup isn't selling a commercial break—it's selling the game itself. Throwing stadium pyrotechnics and international pop stars at it assumes the audience came for both, when really they came for one.

That said, the lineup isn't accidental. Shakira performed at the 2010 South Africa World Cup halftime and is genuinely embedded in soccer culture now. The Coldplay and BTS additions pull younger viewers who might not care about the match but will watch for the artists. Bieber is the wild card, the one that screams "we want everyone." It's not a bad instinct for maximizing viewers—it's just a different game than what die-hard soccer fans showed up to play. Whether that's evolution or miscalculation depends entirely on who's actually sitting in those stadiums and how much they tolerate the interruption.
