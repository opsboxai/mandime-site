A pile-up on the highway usually doesn't start with two cars hitting each other. It starts with one crash, then another driver slamming on the brakes, then a third driver who didn't see the brake lights in time, then a fourth who was going 75 and couldn't stop. That cascade is the real killer, and it's exactly what makes secondary accidents so much worse than the initial impact.

Michael Frank looked into this at Jalopnik, and the math is blunt. When a crash happens on a busy highway, the first collision is often survivable. People walk away. But the secondary pileup that follows, born from panic braking and reduced visibility, stacks up multiple impacts at speed with no warning. A driver might see brake lights two cars ahead, but not the stopped vehicle directly in front of them. Reaction time collapses. By the time someone realizes they need to stop, they're already committed.

The conditions make it exponentially worse. Reduced visibility from smoke or debris means following drivers have seconds, not minutes, to react. Speeds remain dangerously high because upstream traffic hasn't received the signal yet. And unlike a controlled collision where people brace for impact, secondary accident victims don't know it's coming. Your body doesn't prepare. The damage compounds.

This is why traffic authorities clear crash scenes fast, why highway patrol blocks lanes immediately, and why highway safety barriers exist. One bad moment becomes a catastrophe only if the cascade is allowed to continue. The initial crash is bad. The one that follows is what sends people to the hospital or the morgue.
