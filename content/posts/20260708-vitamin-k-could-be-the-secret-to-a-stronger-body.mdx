If you've ever wondered why you bruise easily or why small cuts won't stop bleeding, you might be low on vitamin K. This unsung hero of vitamins is crucial for blood clotting and bone health, but it's often overlooked. Dr. Hasaneen Al Janabi, a leading expert, explains that vitamin K, especially vitamin K2, is gaining attention for its role in bone quality, cardiovascular health, and longevity.

Vitamin K is a fat-soluble vitamin, meaning it's absorbed through dietary fats. There are two types: K1, which supports blood clotting and is found in green leafy vegetables, and K2, which helps direct calcium to bones and teeth, preventing it from accumulating in soft tissues. This makes K2 particularly important for bone and cardiovascular health. 

Without enough vitamin K, your body can't properly activate proteins involved in blood clotting, bone formation, and maintaining healthy tissues. This can lead to easy bruising, frequent nosebleeds, and slow wound healing. If you're a fit adult who eats greens, you're probably fine, but those with conditions affecting fat absorption, like celiac disease or Crohn’s disease, might be at risk.

The general advice is to consume about 1 microgram of vitamin K per kilogram of body weight daily. Most people can meet this through a balanced diet, but supplementation might be beneficial for some. Just remember to consult your doctor before starting any new supplement, especially if you're on anticoagulant medications like Warfarin.

In short, vitamin K isn't just about blood clotting; it's a key player in maintaining strong bones and a healthy heart. So, load up on those leafy greens and consider a supplement if needed. Your body will thank you.
