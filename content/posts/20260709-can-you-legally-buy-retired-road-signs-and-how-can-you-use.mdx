If you've ever dreamed of hanging a retired road sign on your wall, you're in luck, but proceed with caution. The legality of buying and displaying these signs can vary wildly depending on where you live. For instance, in Texas, stealing a sign can land you in jail for at least two years, while in Michigan, it's a misdemeanor with a max of one year in the pokey. 

The key is to ensure the sign is officially retired. The U.S. Department of Transportation's Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices provides guidelines for when a sign should be decommissioned, often due to wear and tear or damage. Once retired, these signs can end up on auction sites like govdeals.com, where you can find everything from bullet-riddled stop signs to speed limit markers from various cities.

But here's the catch: you can own a retired sign, but displaying it as an official traffic control device is a no-go. In New York, for example, doing so can get you 15 days in jail, and in Georgia, it's a $1,000 fine or up to a year in jail. So, hang that 65 mph sign inside your garage, not on your front lawn. 

In the end, it's a legal minefield, but with a bit of research and caution, you can add a unique piece of road history to your collection without ending up behind bars.
