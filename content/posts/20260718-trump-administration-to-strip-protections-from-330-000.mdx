The Trump administration just green-lit mining operations within 10 miles of Chaco Culture National Historic Park, which means 330,000 acres of protected public land in New Mexico are about to open up for extraction. This isn't a small zoning tweak. Chaco is one of the most archaeologically significant sites in North America, home to thousand-year-old pueblo ruins and sacred ground for Navajo and other tribes. The buffer zone that existed was supposed to keep heavy industry away from it. That's gone now.

What makes this particularly stark is the timing and the math. The protections were put in place specifically because mining operations so close to Chaco would degrade the landscape, contaminate water sources, and trash sites that can't be replaced. This is New Mexico, which means coal and uranium mining operations. You're talking about industrial infrastructure, truck traffic, dust, and chemical runoff in proximity to irreplaceable cultural heritage. The tribes and archaeologists who've fought to keep Chaco intact have said repeatedly that once you mine it, it's done. You don't get a second take.

The argument from the administration's side is predictable: energy independence, jobs, revenue. The counterargument is that some things shouldn't have a price tag attached to them. If you believe public lands should stay public and that indigenous heritage sites deserve actual protection instead of theater, this one lands hard. Whether it holds up legally is another question entirely, but the message is clear: if you weren't paying attention to what happens to protected lands under this administration, you probably should be now.
