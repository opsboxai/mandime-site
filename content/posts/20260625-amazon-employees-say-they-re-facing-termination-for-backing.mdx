three Amazon software engineers testified at Seattle City Council hearings about data centers, and now they're facing potential termination. The engineers, Patrick Schloesser, Darius Irani, and Liesl Wigand, spoke up in favor of regulating data centers, only to be called into meetings with HR just days after their testimony. 

The engineers cited Seattle’s law that bars employment discrimination over political speech, but they claim Amazon is retaliating against them for their public stance. On June 10th, one week after the hearing and a day after the City Council passed a moratorium on data centers, each engineer was summoned to a meeting with Amazon’s “Employee Relations” team. HR representatives informed them that the company was investigating their actions and that disciplinary action, including termination, could follow.

Schloesser told The Verge, “I am unwilling to accept a reality in which Amazon or any corporation can silence me in exercising my rights. We’re not going to step back in line.” The engineers are now seeking legal action, filing a complaint with the Seattle Office for Civil Rights, alleging that Amazon engaged in prohibited employment discrimination.

Amazon disputes these claims, stating that employees are free to discuss their working environment but must follow certain procedures when speaking on behalf of the company. The company also denied having plans to terminate the employees, saying, “It’s inaccurate to say that we have plans to terminate these employees or told them they were at risk of termination.”

The engineers are members of Amazon Employees for Climate Justice (AECJ), a group advocating for climate action. Last year, they published an open letter signed by over 1,000 Amazon employees, urging the company to power all its data centers with 100 percent additional, local renewable energy.

Schloesser described the HR meeting as a “foreboding” experience, where he felt pressured to admit to something he hadn’t done. Irani echoed similar sentiments, saying he felt “rattled and unsure of myself” after the meeting, adding that the HR representative seemed to be waiting for him to admit to wrongdoing.

The legal complaint filed by AECJ requests that the Seattle Office for Civil Rights investigate these allegations and take action to remedy any unlawful discrimination. Abby Lawlor, AECJ’s counsel, emphasized that Seattle’s law protects employees from discrimination based on their political beliefs and the organizations they belong to. 

This case highlights the tension between corporate policies and employees’ rights to speak out on political issues. The engineers are not backing down, and their fight could set a precedent for how tech workers can advocate for their beliefs without fear of retaliation.
