Big Tech is throwing billions into America’s data center buildout, creating a surge of opportunities for electricians. But as national opposition to these facilities grows, some workers are questioning whether it’s worth it.

The International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers (IBEW) has argued that its workers are “powering the AI Revolution,” and a set of “Data Center Principles” published in March argues that union labor is “essential to the future of AI.” Tech giants like Meta and Google are jumping on board, committing millions to training programs for skilled trades. Yet, amid this boom, debates over the ethics of data center work have started to emerge within the electrician community.

On r/electricians, a subreddit with around half a million monthly visitors, threads about AI’s impact on the economy are popping up. Some electricians worry that their work could lead to widespread job losses or that they’re complicit in harming local communities. For others, it’s a clear-cut issue: work is work.

One electrician based in the Midwest says he no longer tells people what he does for a living. “The conversation shifts or gets shut down altogether” when he reveals his line of work. He’s faced instances where people told him “how terrible it is that you’re contributing to something like that.” Despite these concerns, he sought out data center work, seeing it as a unique opportunity for upward mobility. He was hired as an electrician but quickly promoted to a management role, with hopes of transitioning into an engineering role.

An electrician named Ryan, who has never worked at a data center and probably never will, sees it differently. He’s wary of corporate greed and believes that executives like Elon Musk and Alex Karp are “suspicious at best.” Ryan, an IBEW worker, has some agency over his work and can say no to jobs he deems unethical. He would rather avoid data center work, even if it means being out of work for a long time.

Jesse, another IBEW electrician, acknowledges community pushback but believes that workers shouldn’t be judged for taking on data center work. He argues that these issues should be addressed by contacting state and local governments, not by blaming electricians who need the work.

An electrician named Dante, who has worked on data centers operated by Intel, HP, and Amazon, sees it as a matter of survival. “We're almost always working for the worst possible people in the end, but we all need a paycheck because of the unlivable world that those same rich people have created for us,” he says. For him, it’s all about the paycheck, regardless of the project.

For others, this rationalization doesn’t cut it. One electrician tells WIRED that job scarcity can fuel an attitude that since workers “have to put food on the table,” they should be immune to criticism. Pushing back against this mindset, they say, “would not end well at a union hall.” But privately, they take issue with it.

The tension between ethical concerns and economic necessity is palpable. As data centers continue to grow, the debate over whether to work on them or not will likely persist. For many electricians, it’s a tough call, balancing their livelihood with their moral compass.
