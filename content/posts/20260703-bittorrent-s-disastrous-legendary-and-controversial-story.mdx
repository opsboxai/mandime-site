Twenty-five years ago, Bram Cohen unleashed BitTorrent, a file-sharing app that would become the bane of Hollywood's existence. Cohen's creation quickly became a cornerstone of internet piracy, with some estimates suggesting it was responsible for up to one-third of all internet traffic in 2004. But what makes BitTorrent's story so compelling isn't just its impact on piracy; it's the ingenious way Cohen designed it to dodge legal repercussions.

Cohen's approach was to strip BitTorrent down to its core functionality: swarming distribution. This meant dividing files into tiny chunks and allowing users to trade them in large swarms, ensuring that no single user had to download an entire file from one source. The app's lack of search functionality and reliance on third-party trackers made it nearly impossible for authorities to pin down exactly what was being shared. Cohen wasn't just building a tool; he was starting a revolution.

The entertainment industry's attempts to shut down other file-sharing services like Napster and Kazaa were futile against BitTorrent. Cohen's design ensured that the app itself couldn't be held liable for the content shared on it. This legal shield allowed BitTorrent to thrive, becoming a go-to platform for everything from anime fans to jam band enthusiasts.

But the real kicker is that Cohen wasn't trying to start a business; he was trying to change the game. "My plan was not to start a business. My plan was to start a revolution," Cohen said. And that's exactly what he did. BitTorrent's legacy is a testament to the power of decentralized technology and the enduring allure of sharing. Whether you're a fan of Phish bootlegs or just someone who appreciates a good tech story, BitTorrent's journey is one for the books.
