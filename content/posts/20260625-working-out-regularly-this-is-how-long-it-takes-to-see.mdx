For the average guy hitting the gym, progress can feel like a mystery wrapped in a riddle. But the truth is, there’s a method to the madness, and it’s all about understanding how your body responds to new stimuli. Dean Stattmann breaks down the science of seeing results, and it’s not as complicated as you might think.

When you first start working out, the gains come fast. David Ayotte, a strength and endurance coach, explains that beginners can see changes within the first two to three weeks. This initial progress is a result of your body adapting to new movements and breaking down tissue. Whether you’re lifting weights or running, the body responds quickly to a new stimulus. But as you progress, the gains naturally slow down. Your body adapts, and you hit a plateau.

To keep pushing through those plateaus, trainers recommend breaking your training into blocks, typically around 12 to 16 weeks. Each block should introduce new challenges, whether it’s changing rep schemes, trying new exercises, or setting fresh goals. Ayotte suggests running people on 12-week cycles, adding progressions like switching from dumbbell squats to barbell squats to break through plateaus.

The key to seeing meaningful results is to train hard. Ayotte emphasizes that most people don’t push themselves enough to see real change. Whether you’re lifting weights or running, you need to push to failure or close to it. This intensity is crucial for muscle growth and improving your running times. After two months of consistent, hard training, seasoned lifters and runners can expect to see significant gains.

Training blocks not only keep your body guessing but also provide a mental reset. Ben Parker, co-founder and head coach at Runna, notes that this approach keeps training interesting and helps maintain motivation. Over a broader timeline, you’ll see consistent progress, even if it’s not always linear. Parker points out that while progress can be slow, it’s important to account for the risk of injury, which can set you back.

For runners, Ayotte suggests that if you’re focusing on recovery and dialing in your paces, you can see a five to 10-second drop in your easy run pace per week. However, tracking progress on a weekly basis can be misleading. Some goals, like building muscle, take longer to see in the mirror compared to adding heavier weights to the bar. Ayotte notes that strength gains often come before visible muscle growth.

In the end, progress is about consistency and pushing yourself. Whether you’re a beginner or a seasoned athlete, breaking your training into blocks and consistently challenging yourself will keep you moving forward. So, next time you hit the gym, remember: it’s not just about the reps, it’s about the journey.
