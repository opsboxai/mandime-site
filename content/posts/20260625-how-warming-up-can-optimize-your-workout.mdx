Committing to warming up and cooling down properly is far more important than gutting out that last rep. David Taylor breaks down why these routines are essential to optimizing your workout.

If you’ve ever skipped warm-up and cool-down exercises at the gym because you were in a rush, you’re not alone. But these routines are crucial for getting the most out of your workout. A well-done warm-up can make the difference between a slow, sore session and an efficient, effective one.

Lotti Sorrell, founder of London-based personal training and wellness studio Longevity Fitness, emphasizes the importance of priming your body for movement. "Not only are you switching on muscles that need to be ready to work during your session, but you’re also activating your nervous system, which can help you hit or even surpass your targets."

Personal trainer Lewis Paris likens a proper warm-up to letting the shower run for a few seconds before getting in. "Giving time to allow your body to be prepped and primed should be a priority," he explains. "Warming up helps reduce the risk of injury by contracting the muscles you want to work, releasing synovial fluid to lubricate the joints, and giving you time to mentally prepare."

Jason Smith, founder of Fit in Midlife, adds that a good warm-up gradually activates muscles, increases blood flow, and prepares joints, making your body ready for higher-intensity workouts. "This means you’ll get more from your workout and can reduce the dreaded post-workout DOMS," he says.

After your workout, a cool-down is almost as important as a warm-up. It eases your body out of exercise mode and gives your mind some time to decompress. "Would you jump out of a moving car or do you ensure the car can be stopped appropriately so you can get out safely? I presume the latter!" says Paris. "Allowing time to bring the heart rate back down to pre-exercise levels is important, especially for those who have exerted themselves quite heavily or been diagnosed with a medical condition such as diabetes or high blood pressure."

Tyrone Brennand, a personal trainer and author of Be The Fittest, found tangible gains from adding cool-downs to his routine. "One of the first ways I tried to get more mobile and flexible was by simply stretching out after the workout," he says. "During workouts, you push the muscles to the point of exhaustion, and they tighten up. Cool-downs help with recovery."

Any warm-up should be tailored to the workout, with heavier sessions needing more prep. A good rule of thumb is between five and 20 minutes, according to the experts. "Slowly ramp up your movements throughout this time, so that by the end of your preparation segment your workload should blend seamlessly into your main session," Sorrell says.

Cool-downs are typically shorter, lasting between five and 10 minutes. "The main focus is to bring the heart rate back down to rest," says Paris. "So if you have high blood pressure, more time may be needed to bring you back down to normal levels."

For cardiovascular work, warming up is as simple as getting moving. Mimicking the movements you'll employ during your workout is the most efficient way of preparing your body. "You see this a lot with soccer players or people that do track work," says Brennand. "Before they go onto the field, you see them running up and down, trying to move the body in certain directions to warm up."

Smith agrees with Brennand when it comes to knee lifts, and also suggests quick warm-up movements like star jumps, running on the spot, or an incline treadmill walk, gradually increasing pace and incline.

For strength training, focus on incorporating the movements you'll be running through at full capacity later on. "Perform this movement pattern without weight first, then bring in some element of balance work to get your nervous system firing," says Sorrell.

When it comes to full-body and weight sessions, slow and steady is the warm-up key. Take everything down in tempo and weight: slow bodyweight squats, slow press-ups, and shoulder rotations are some of Smith's suggestions.

Remember to base your warm-up on the workout. "If it's a full-body workout, I start by opening up the spine," Brennand says. "A lot of people, when they first wake up, feel they've got a sore or tight back. I do some spinal twists to open up, then I go through my specific needs."

By committing to a proper warm-up and cool-down, you’ll not only optimize your workout but also reduce the risk of injury and improve your overall performance.
