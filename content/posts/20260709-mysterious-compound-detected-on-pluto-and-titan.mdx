Something on Pluto and Titan is absorbing light in a way that has scientists scratching their heads. The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) detected a chemical signature centered at 5.113 micrometers on both worlds, and it doesn’t match anything in spectroscopic databases. This isn’t a fluke; the signal appeared in observations made with two different instruments, ruling out any calibration issues.

The mystery deepens when you consider the vastly different environments of Pluto and Titan. Titan has a dense atmosphere rich in nitrogen and methane, with liquid methane rivers and lakes, while Pluto is a frigid, airless world with a thin atmosphere of nitrogen, methane, and carbon monoxide. Despite these differences, both bodies show the same unexplained chemical signature. This suggests that the compound could be a result of complex organic chemistry driven by solar radiation and cosmic rays, leading to the formation of new compounds that settle on their surfaces.

Researchers are now exploring the possibility that this signature comes from a known compound in a state or mixture never before studied in the lab. They’re also considering the chance that it could be a completely new compound whose chemistry has yet to be characterized. Solving this puzzle will require more observations from the JWST and experiments to recreate the chemistry of these icy worlds. NASA’s Dragonfly mission to Titan might provide some clues, even if it can’t directly observe the infrared signature. The hunt for this mysterious compound is set to become one of the most intriguing puzzles in planetary science.
