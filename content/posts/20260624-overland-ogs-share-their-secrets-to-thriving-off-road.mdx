Overland OGs Share Their Secrets to Thriving Off-Road

Overland Expo West isn’t just about the latest gear; it’s a gathering of seasoned overlanders with decades of experience. These Overland OGs have seen it all and have some hard-earned advice to share. The key takeaway? Less is more, and you don’t need a fancy rig to hit the road.

Raquel Hernández, who runs the Instagram account @yogaslackers with her partner Sam Salwei, advises newbies to start small. "Go and do it with what you have, or build your rig very slowly," she says. "Do a third of the upgrades you think you want to do, go use it, figure out what you actually like or don’t like, before you go out and spend a lot of money on something you regret."

Lesa NeSmith, who has been car camping since 1978, drives an affordable 1998 Chevy Tracker and doesn’t feel the need to keep up with the latest gear. "I buy some of my stuff from Walmart. If I can figure it out, if I can go [on adventures], you can go. Just go do it," she says.

David Jenden, who has been adventuring since 1988, echoes this sentiment. "You don’t have to have a brand new super shiny rig. You can actually go out and have a really fun time in a $2,500 old pickup truck with a tent in the back and an old mattress," he says. Jenden also advises sticking to analog navigation. "I like to look at a proper map and go, ‘Oh, that looks like a really cool road, I’m going to drive up there. And then of course there are always stunning views and all the fun things that happen on the way."

Allan Ivy and his wife Fancie, who started overlanding in 1980, advise not letting a vehicle be a barrier. "My only advice is don’t look back. Get out there with whatever you have at your disposal," Allan says.

Overland OGs also stress the importance of self-reliance. Stan Dietzman, who’s been off-roading since 1971, says, "Get comfortable with helping yourself out of a hard spot. Don’t rely on others, because that’s kind of setting yourself up for failure." Steve and Sioux Tobueren, who’ve been adventuring together for 50 years, take a different approach. "Be conservative. Turn around instead of pushing the limits," Steve says. "If you want your wife to keep going with you, turn around," adds Sioux.

Bradley Enlow, who built out his off-road 1982 Toyota Sunrader, found that meeting people is part of the adventure. "People pull up with their 40-foot fifth-wheels with their generators going, and you think, ‘Take off, get out of there.’ But when I stay, I end up meeting some of the most genuine and incredible people," he says.

Jeff and Kristi Hosea, who’ve been overlanding since 1994, emphasize flexibility and adaptability. "You have to be flexible and adaptable out on the road. Don’t panic," Jeff says. Kristi adds, "Have a daily ritual. I think it’s important to kind of ground yourself every day… practice a little mindfulness. It helps set you up for whatever you might encounter that day, because you don’t know most of the time."

These OGs have seen it all and their advice is invaluable. Start small, stay self-reliant, and embrace the unpredictability of off-road life.
