President Trump just signed an order slashing nearly 3 million acres from Bears Ears and Grand Staircase-Escalante national monuments in Utah. The move reduces Bears Ears to about 121,000 acres and Grand Staircase-Escalante to around 182,000 acres, leaving them at a fraction of their originally designated sizes.

This decision is a stark reminder of the political tug-of-war over public lands. Utah Senator Mike Lee praised the move, arguing that presidents have been using monument designations to lock up millions of acres and restrict local access. But this order is a slap in the face to Native American tribes and conservationists who see these lands as irreplaceable cultural and ecological treasures.

The Bears Ears Commission, made up of five Tribal Nations, fought hard to protect these areas, recognizing the deep cultural and historical significance. The monuments are not just museums; they are living landscapes that hold immense value for Indigenous communities. The Trump administration's decision to slash these areas is not just about land; it's about disregarding the voices and rights of those who have lived on and cared for these lands for generations.

Conservationists and lawmakers are already gearing up to fight this move, arguing that these public lands should be protected, not handed over to drilling and mining interests. The battle over these monuments is far from over, and it's clear that the fight to protect these remarkable places will continue.
