The Pentagon's new defense secretary Pete Hegseth has long been vocal about testosterone optimization, posting about it on social media and promoting the ideology to his millions of followers. Now that he's running military personnel decisions, the manosphere's obsession with male hormones and "biohacking" isn't just fringe internet noise anymore. It's policy.

Victoria Song's reporting on this tracks how testosterone-boosting supplements, wellness claims, and the broader "optimizer" mentality have moved from Reddit threads and influencer TikToks straight into the halls of the Department of Defense. The thing is, most of these products don't do what they claim. A lot of testosterone supplements are either underdosed, contaminated, or just expensive placebos. The science is thin. But the fervor around them is real, and it's shaped by an entire ecosystem of guys selling confidence wrapped in pseudoscience.

The actual risk here isn't hard to see. If military decisions start getting filtered through a lens where physical optimization and testosterone levels become markers of military readiness or leadership quality, you're making policy based on internet bro logic instead of evidence. That's the real problem. Not that guys want to feel better. But that when ideology meets institutional power, people further down the chain start living with the consequences.
