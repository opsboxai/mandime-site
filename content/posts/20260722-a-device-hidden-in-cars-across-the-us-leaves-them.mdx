Millions of cars rolling around the US have an aftermarket alarm system installed by dealerships that can be remotely hacked to unlock doors, track location, and kill the engine. The vulnerability isn't theoretical. Researchers found that these devices, typically installed without explicit buyer consent and often left active even after customers disabled them through the infotainment system, respond to basic radio commands with virtually no authentication. That means someone with the right equipment and knowledge can compromise your car from outside your garage.

The real problem is the install-and-forget model. Dealerships slap these alarms on vehicles as add-ons, sometimes bundled into financing packages customers don't fully understand. Even when owners think they've turned the system off, the hardware stays live and networked. It's the kind of supply chain security blind spot that tends to persist until someone documents it publicly, which is exactly what happened here. Andy Greenberg reported on the research, and now the question is whether dealers and manufacturers actually push fixes or whether this becomes one more invisible vulnerability that stays embedded in the fleet.

The patch exists. Manufacturers can push firmware updates that require actual authentication for remote commands instead of responding to any radio signal in range. But that requires coordination between researchers, manufacturers, dealers, and owners who may not even know they have the device. If you bought a car from a dealership in the last few years and never had an explicit conversation about adding an alarm system, you might be carrying one anyway. Worth checking your paperwork or asking your dealer directly. The window to fix this at scale exists right now. After a few years, it gets harder.
