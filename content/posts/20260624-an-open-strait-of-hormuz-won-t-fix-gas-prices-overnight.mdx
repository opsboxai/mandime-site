Even if peace holds between the US and Iran, oil prices aren’t set to plummet anytime soon. On Wednesday, President Trump signed a Memorandum of Understanding with Iran, officially ending a 110-day conflict and reopening the Strait of Hormuz, a critical oil shipping route. By Thursday, 10 vessels that had been stranded for months began moving out of the area. But experts warn that US consumers shouldn’t expect gas prices to drop back to pre-war levels any time soon.

The Strait of Hormuz, a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman, usually sees about 20 million barrels of oil moving through daily. Despite the ceasefire, the strait remains seeded with mines, and shippers are still wary of the tenuous peace. The machinery of oil production is only just starting to get back to normal, and the global supply-demand balance has been severely disrupted.

Jason Miller, a professor of supply chain management at Michigan State University, notes that “there's no sign that prices are heading back to February levels just yet.” Consumers should budget for higher prices for the foreseeable future. The situation is incredibly fragile, and the war has permanently transformed global shipping dynamics.

Jakob Larsen, the chief safety and security officer at BIMCO, the world’s largest international shipping organization, warns that the strait remains a safety risk. Routes have to be de-mined, and it’s unclear how long that process will take. The effort could involve several countries, minesweeping ships, underwater drones, and even mine-detecting dolphins.

The war may have also created a new precedent. Iran could impose tolls on ships passing through the strait, adding extra costs and potentially leading to higher prices for consumers. This could set a dangerous model for other countries to follow, creating chaos in global shipping.

Petroleum-based products, like plastic packaging, will likely remain pricier for at least the next month or two. The best-case scenario is that the pace of inflation slows, but it’s still a faster pace than before the war. The oil crunch hasn’t been as dire as initially predicted, partly due to ample global crude oil inventories and China’s significant reduction in oil imports.

In the coming weeks, what matters most will be how many ships enter the Persian Gulf to load up with oil. The oil industry’s recovery will determine how much the average consumer will have to spend on everyday products. The situation remains volatile, and it’s unclear when production can fully restart in the region. 

For guys who are into cars, gaming, and tech, this means higher costs for fuel and potentially more expensive gadgets and electronics. The global supply chain is still in recovery mode, and it’s going to take some time before things get back to normal.
