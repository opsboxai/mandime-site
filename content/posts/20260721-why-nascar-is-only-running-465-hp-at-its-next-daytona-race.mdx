NASCAR just dialed back the horsepower on its biggest stages, and the reason matters more than the number itself.

Starting with the August Daytona race, stock cars will run 465 hp instead of the usual 550 hp on high-speed ovals. That's less power than a base Mustang GT. The catch: it's intentional, part of a broader rules package designed to make the racing actually watchable again. When cars run flat out at Daytona and Talladega, the aerodynamic advantages compound so heavily that passing becomes nearly impossible. Drivers get sucked into a single-file parade, following the car ahead with zero chance of overtaking unless someone crashes or runs out of fuel. The racing gets boring fast, which is the opposite of what you want on the circuit's two fastest tracks.

The power reduction forces drivers to stay on the throttle longer through turns instead of hitting the straights at full tilt, which theoretically breaks up the aero advantage and creates more passing opportunities. It's a Band-Aid on a bigger problem: stock cars have become so dependent on aerodynamic grip that they've lost the raw mechanical drama of racing. Less power means more reliance on braking, turn-in, and driver skill to separate the field. Whether it actually works depends on how the cars stack up in real-world conditions, but the concept is sound. NASCAR has tried this before with mixed results, so August at Daytona will either prove the fix works or send the sanctioning body back to the drawing board.
