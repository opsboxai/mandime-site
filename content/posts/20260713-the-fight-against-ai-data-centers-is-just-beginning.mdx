Years before the AI boom threatened local power grids, a small group of protesters in Ireland set the stage for the battles cropping up across communities today. In 2015, Apple announced plans to build a $1 billion data center in the sleepy town of Athenry, Ireland. Despite Apple’s promises of renewable energy and community benefits, local residents lodged complaints about noise, light pollution, and environmental impacts. The fight dragged on for years, with Apple eventually giving up in 2018 after facing legal challenges and community opposition.

Fast forward to 2026, and the same scenario is playing out across the US and beyond. Data centers, especially those for AI, are consuming as much energy as entire states, and residents are pushing back. From January to March alone, protesters have blocked or delayed at least 75 projects in the US valued at $130 billion. The number of active opposition groups has more than doubled, now spanning 49 states.

The stakes are high. The US Energy Information Administration predicts that commercial energy demand will surpass residential demand for the first time this year, largely due to AI data centers. That demand is expected to double by 2027. Residents are seeing rising energy costs, water quality issues, and environmental concerns. In Wisconsin, QTS dropped plans for a $12 billion data center campus after community protests. In Delaware, a planned data center is facing roadblocks due to state regulations. And in Virginia, opponents successfully blocked a QTS data center in Prince William County.

As the fight against AI data centers intensifies, a political battle is also brewing in Congress. President Trump’s executive order to fast-track data center construction is facing opposition from lawmakers who want to protect residents from utility price hikes and environmental harm. Bills like the Ratepayer Protection Act and the GRID Act aim to regulate data center energy usage and costs, while local governments are enacting their own laws to curb the impact of these facilities.

The fight is far from over, and with new data center proposals emerging each week, communities are gearing up for a long battle to protect their homes and resources.
