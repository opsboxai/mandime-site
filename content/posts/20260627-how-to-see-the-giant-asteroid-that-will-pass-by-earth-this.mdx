This weekend, a giant asteroid named (152637) 1997 NC1 will make its closest approach to Earth in 400 years. No, it won’t hit us, but it will be visible through commercial telescopes and astronomical binoculars. The asteroid, with a diameter between 700 meters and 1.6 kilometers, will pass 2.56 million kilometers (1.59 million miles) from Earth on Saturday, June 27, at 11:14 UTC. That’s about 6.6 times the distance between Earth and the moon.

The best time to view it depends on your location. The asteroid will remain visible for several days after its closest approach, though it will gradually become fainter. It will be near the constellations Ophiuchus and Serpens Cauda, south of the bright star Vega. 

To spot it, you’ll need more than just your eyes. The asteroid is about 40 times fainter than the faintest stars visible to the naked eye. A commercial telescope with an aperture of at least 100 millimeters (4 inches) is recommended, though models ranging from 150 to 200 millimeters will provide a better viewing experience. Astronomical binoculars with a magnification of 15 x 70 or 20 x 80, mounted on a tripod, can also work, especially from a location away from light pollution.

The asteroid will appear as a small point of light moving slowly among the stars. Its movement will be about 40 arc seconds per minute, which is noticeable if you watch it for a few minutes. Astronomy apps like Stellarium, Sky Tonight, or SkySafari can help you locate it in the sky.

If you can’t get outside or don’t have the right equipment, the Virtual Telescope Project is hosting livestreams on June 26 and June 27. So, whether you’re a seasoned stargazer or just curious about celestial events, this is a rare opportunity to witness a piece of cosmic history.
