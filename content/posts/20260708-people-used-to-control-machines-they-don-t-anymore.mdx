Driving a stick shift used to be a rite of passage, a tangible connection to the mechanics of the machine. But in just over a decade, the manual transmission has gone from a staple to a rarity. In 2000, CarMax reported that 15 percent of its cars were stick shifts. By 2020, that number had plummeted to 2.4 percent. Even brands like Mercedes and Volkswagen are phasing out the manual transmission globally.

This shift isn’t just about driving; it’s about our relationship with the physical world. As Ian Bogost points out, the decline of the manual transmission mirrors a broader trend of automation and digitalization. From cars to appliances, we’re losing touch with the sensory experience of operating things manually. The stick shift represents a direct, tactile connection to the machine, something that’s increasingly rare in our digital age.

Philosopher Matthew Crawford argues that this loss of manual control isn’t just about driving; it’s about feeling fully human. Operating a manual transmission, or even sending a postcard, connects us to the world in a way that digital interactions can’t. The postcard Bogost received, a relic from 1994, is a poignant reminder of a time when physical objects held more significance. In a world regulated by devices, we’ve become disconnected from the tangible, sensory experiences that once gave our lives rich meaning.
