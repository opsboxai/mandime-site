Taylor Farms, one of the largest produce suppliers in the US, cut a $1 million check to MAGA Inc., the super PAC backing Donald Trump, while simultaneously spending millions more lobbying against food safety regulations. Then came the diarrhea outbreak.

Between 2020 and 2025, the California-based company donated over $3.6 million to conservative groups and causes, with a clear pattern: they were funding political efforts to weaken the very regulations designed to catch exactly what happened in their supply chain. The timing is brutal. While they were fighting food safety rules, the outbreak tied to their salad products sickened people across multiple states. It's the kind of irony that doesn't need commentary—it speaks for itself.

What makes this story stick isn't just the hypocrisy, though that's part of it. It's that food safety regulation exists because of previous outbreaks that killed people. Taylor Farms' lobbying efforts weren't abstract political positioning—they were direct opposition to the kind of oversight that, when done right, prevents public health disasters. The company has faced recalls before. They knew the stakes. And they still chose to invest heavily in candidates and groups working to dial back enforcement.

The outbreak is a live case study in why regulatory capture matters. When companies successfully lobby to weaken the rules meant to protect consumers, the cost isn't paid by lobbyists or executives in a boardroom. It's paid by the person in an emergency room on a Saturday night wondering why they can't keep food down. Taylor Farms is learning that lesson in real time, and so is everyone who got sick from their product.
