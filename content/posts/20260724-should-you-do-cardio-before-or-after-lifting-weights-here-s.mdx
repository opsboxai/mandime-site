The real question isn't when to do cardio. It's what you actually want to get better at.

If your primary goal is building strength and muscle, lift first. Your central nervous system and muscles are freshest at the start of your workout, which means you'll move heavier weight with better form and actually make progress on lifts that matter. Cardio after doesn't kill your gains, but it does come after you've already extracted the most demanding work from your body. The math is simple: your best effort goes toward your priority. If you're chasing a deadlift PR or trying to add plate to your bench, you can't afford to spend your peak energy capacity on a treadmill.

The opposite is true if endurance is your target. Runners and cyclists need their top-end performance for actual running and cycling, not as a warm-up to weight training. Do cardio when you're sharp, then hit weights afterward as a strength maintenance tool. Most endurance athletes who lift aren't trying to become bodybuilders anyway, so the order makes sense.

Where people get confused is the middle ground. If you're just trying to stay fit and do both moderately well, the order matters less than consistency. A 15-minute warm-up jog before weights won't tank your strength. Light weight training after a run won't wreck your cardio fitness. The difference between optimal and acceptable is real but small. The difference between training with a plan and not training at all is enormous.
