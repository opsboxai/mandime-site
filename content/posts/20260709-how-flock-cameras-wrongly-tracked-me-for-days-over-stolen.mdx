“Are you armed?!” the police officer screamed. “Get out of the car!”

That’s how Joel Feder found himself in a tense standoff with four cop cars in a Kohl’s parking lot in suburban Minnesota. The Range Rover he was testing had been flagged as stolen due to a simple data error, and Flock’s nationwide surveillance camera network had been tracking him for days. The irony? He was just running errands with his wife.

This incident highlights the dark side of our increasingly surveilled society. Flock’s AI-driven license plate cameras, designed to catch criminals, instead turned Joel into a suspect. The system couldn’t handle a minor discrepancy in the plate number, leading to a real-life game of cat and mouse. The tech is supposed to make us safer, but it can also create a Kafkaesque nightmare where you’re guilty until proven innocent.

The bigger question is, how many other innocent people are being flagged and tracked without their knowledge? Flock’s network is expanding, and with it, the potential for more of these edge cases. It’s a chilling reminder that in the surveillance state, the line between safety and privacy is dangerously thin. Welcome to the future, where a simple drive to the store can turn into a police chase.
