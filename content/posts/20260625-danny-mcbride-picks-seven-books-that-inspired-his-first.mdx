Danny McBride, the mastermind behind some of the most cringe-worthy and hilarious characters in modern media, has dipped his toes into the world of literature with his first short-story collection, *Thrilling Tales of Modern Men*. In this collection, McBride delves into the lives of men grappling with the mundane and the absurd, often with a touch of dark humor and a deep understanding of the human condition.

"All these guys are unfulfilled or even disappointments to themselves," McBride explains. "I think that makes them insecure about how they might be letting down the other people in their lives; sometimes they react by putting up some hard shell or by doubling down on their bullshit behavior." McBride's characters are flawed, often making questionable choices, but he sees them as real people trying to figure out their place in the world. "Everyone is just trying to figure out who they are and what they’re doing," he says. "With men my age, we all grew up watching Rambo and now we all work at Geico. It’s like there’s a sense of trying to reconcile what you ended up being with the maybe inflated version of what you thought you were supposed to do from your upbringing, or what you were exposed to on TV and movies as a kid."

One of the books that has heavily influenced McBride's writing is Cormac McCarthy's *Blood Meridian*. "I love this book," McBride admits. "I know it's a difficult book for some people to get through, and I've definitely recommended it to some people who are like, 'You're a sicko. What do you like about this?' But I love it." McCarthy's brutal depiction of the Old West strips away all the heroic myths, revealing the senseless brutality beneath. "It's just about what's underneath all of that stuff. And all underneath that is a worldview that says, manhood is measured by ability to act, withstand, and commit violence. And it's just at its most fucked up and brutal."

Another influential book is Pat Conroy's *The Lords of Discipline*. "This one’s about institutional masculinity. What happens when boys are made into men through cruelty?" McBride asks. "Pat Conroy, I love him, by the way, and now that I live in Charleston, all of it takes on such a different meaning." Conroy's book is a reflection on his time at The Citadel, a military college, and the complex emotions that come with it. "I read that when I was pretty young and it really hit me; the characters spoke to me at the time. It’s also kind of that whole thing of, when you're a kid, growing up, you do want to be a part of something, you want to belong to a higher thing."

McBride's stories in *Thrilling Tales of Modern Men* are a reflection of his deep understanding of masculinity and the struggles men face. From a husband and father pursuing magic in middle age to a man facing the fate of balding, McBride's characters are undeniably 'McBride', flawed, often absurd, but ultimately relatable. "You also realize how short our time is here and that it is going by fast, and you’re not going to live forever," McBride concludes. "It’s the brutalness of reality." Through his stories, McBride offers a glimpse into the lives of modern men, with all their insecurities and contradictions, and the often hilarious and heartbreaking ways they navigate the world.
