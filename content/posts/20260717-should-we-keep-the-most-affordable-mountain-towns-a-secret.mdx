The gatekeeping argument always sounds noble until you actually think about it. Kathleen Rellihan caught flak for naming affordable mountain towns in his column—places where a guy can still buy a house, hike decent trails, and not need six figures to make it work. The pushback was predictable: keep it quiet, don't ruin it, some places should stay hidden. But that's not how towns survive. It's not how communities stay viable.

The real problem isn't that people know about affordable mountain towns. It's that there aren't enough of them. Housing costs in desirable outdoor destinations have become insane because of decades of restrictive zoning, underbuilding, and treating mountain communities like second-home playgrounds for wealthy people. Naming a place doesn't break it. Naming a place might actually be the only thing that forces local governments to build housing instead of watching their young people get priced out entirely. Secret towns don't develop. Secret towns just become richer.

There's also something patronizing about the whole gatekeeping thing. The idea that some places are too special for regular people to know about, that discovery should be earned through obscurity—that's just elitism wrapped in conservation language. A mountain town that can't handle people knowing it exists is a town with a planning problem, not a publicity problem. The towns that thrive are the ones that figure out how to grow thoughtfully, not the ones that stay frozen in amber because someone decided they were too good to share.
