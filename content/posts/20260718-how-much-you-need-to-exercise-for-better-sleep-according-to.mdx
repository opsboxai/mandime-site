The counterintuitive part nobody talks about: more exercise doesn't automatically mean better sleep. There's actually a dose. Researchers have found that around 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week, or 75 minutes of vigorous work, correlates with measurable improvements in sleep quality. But here's the catch. You can also torpedo your sleep by exercising too close to bedtime. That post-workout cortisol spike and elevated heart rate can keep you wired for hours, which is why timing matters more than most people realize.

The real play is consistency and intensity level. A steady routine of moderate activity seems to be where the magic happens for most people, rather than the occasional brutal gym session. Your body adapts to regular patterns, and that regularity signals to your nervous system that it's time to actually rest when bedtime rolls around. Sleep stages deepen, REM sleep increases, and you wake up fewer times during the night. But if you're smashing it at 9pm and then wondering why you're staring at the ceiling at midnight, you've already lost the battle.

Temperature and hormones are the underlying mechanics here. Exercise raises your core body temperature, which then drops as you cool down, and that dip actually triggers sleep onset naturally. The problem is the timing. Get your workout in by early evening, and your body has time to complete that cycle before your head hits the pillow. The standard advice, backed by sleep medicine research, is to finish anything intense at least three to four hours before bed. That leaves room for the stimulation to wear off while your body still benefits from the temperature regulation and the hormonal shifts exercise creates.
