Just over a week ago, I attended a major AI conference in Beijing’s tech district, and the takeaway was clear: the US and China need to put aside their AI rivalry. The event, packed with sessions on everything from recursive self-improvement to humanoid robots, featured AI legends like Whitfield Diffie and Andrew Barto. But the real revelation was the shared concern about the cybersecurity and systemic risks of AI.

Stephen Casper, a computer scientist at MIT, emphasized that AI’s global impact means both the US and China stand to lose if AI development spirals out of control. He likened the current situation to the nuclear arms race, where cooperation was essential despite fierce competition. “AI doesn’t need a Chernobyl moment,” Casper said, highlighting the need for international collaboration to mitigate risks.

One day-long session focused on the cyber challenges posed by advanced AI, including vulnerabilities in AI-generated code and automated social engineering attacks. Lin Yun, a professor at Shanghai Jiao Tong University, noted that while hackers might gain an initial advantage, new countermeasures should eventually tip the balance back toward defense.

The key issue is balancing openness with risk. Chinese companies like Moonshot, Alibaba, and Z.ai have led in offering highly capable open-weight AI models, but as these models advance, ensuring they don’t become cyber weapons becomes increasingly challenging. The latest model from Z.ai, GLM 5.2, includes frontier agentic and coding capabilities, making it as potent as US models like Fable and Mythos.

Yun stressed the need for new ways to guarantee that open models are secure and meet safety standards. A source from a leading Chinese AI company, speaking anonymously, revealed that security concerns are leading some advanced models to be kept closed rather than open source.

The takeaway is clear: both the US and China need to work together to ensure AI development doesn’t spiral into chaos. The stakes are too high for either side to go it alone.
