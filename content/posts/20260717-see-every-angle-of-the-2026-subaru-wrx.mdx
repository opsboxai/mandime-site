Subaru just brought back the base WRX, and the pricing tells you everything about where the market's at right now. Thirty-three grand gets you into the game, which is wild when you consider what you're actually getting: a 271-horsepower flat-four, standard all-wheel drive, and a manual transmission that's still standard equipment, not some boutique option. That's basically free money in 2026.

The refresh is subtle but sharp. They've tightened up the lines, cleaned up the front end, and there's real aggression in the stance without looking like it's trying too hard. The exhaust note is the same signature Subaru boxer thrum you either love or tolerate, but the mechanicals underneath are where it counts. This generation's got better refinement than the last, tighter steering response, and the all-wheel-drive system actually feels like it's working for you instead of just existing. Manual still wins the engagement game over the CVT, which is the only real choice if you're paying attention.

What matters here is that Subaru kept the thing accessible. No turbo nonsense, no six-figure version you're supposed to want. Just a properly sorted sports sedan that'll do 0 to 60 in the low fives and actually makes you feel like you're driving something real. In a segment that's watched the fun get systematized and corporate, that counts for something.
