At the recent "The Briefing: AI for Science" event, Anthropic announced Claude Science, a new AI workbench for scientists, and made waves by saying it would develop drugs of its own. This move puts Anthropic in a unique position, not just selling software to other drugmakers but also competing with them directly. But let's break down what this actually means.

Anthropic, already a powerhouse in AI with its popular coding tools and powerful models, is now eyeing the pharmaceutical industry. The company plans to focus on "neglected" diseases, a noble but ambitious goal. The idea is to use AI to accelerate drug discovery, from finding new compounds to improving them and supporting research. But here's the catch: while AI can speed up the initial stages, it's still a long way from an AI-designed drug reaching patients.

Experts like Namshik Han and Matthew Todd point out that AI is already deeply integrated into drug discovery, but it's far from making experiments unnecessary. The real-world testing of drug candidates for efficacy, toxicity, and practical properties still requires a lot of human input, money, and time. Clinical trials, in particular, are a major bottleneck, with many promising candidates failing at this stage.

So, while Anthropic's move is exciting, it's important to temper expectations. Developing a drug is a complex, lengthy process, and even with cutting-edge AI, it's likely to take years before we see any tangible results. But hey, if Anthropic can crack the code, it could be a game-changer for neglected diseases. Let's keep an eye on this one.
