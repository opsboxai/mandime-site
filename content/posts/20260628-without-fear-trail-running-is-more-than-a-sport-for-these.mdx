Gabe Marrero used to think trail running was a “white people activity.” But after joining the Freedom to Run program by the Running Industry Diversity Coalition (RIDC), he discovered a whole new world. Marrero, along with Yolanda Lopez-Haugabook, formed Sin Miedo, a BIPOC trail running group in Nashville. The name translates to “without fear,” a powerful statement in a place where fear can be a constant.

The idea of trail running in the woods was daunting for Marrero. He worried about getting lost, encountering snakes, and the very real fear of being alone as a person of color. But with the support of RIDC, Marrero and Lopez-Haugabook found a sense of community and safety in nature. They started inviting others to join, and soon Sin Miedo was born.

Trail running is growing, but it’s still overwhelmingly white. According to the American Trail Running Association, 84% of trail runners identify as white. RIDC is working to change that by providing coaching, gear, and community support to people of color. Lopez-Haugabook and her husband realized that running on nature trails was a different experience from running on concrete. It was a chance to connect with something ancestral, something the road could never provide.

Sin Miedo isn’t just about running; it’s about safety and community. In the face of immigration raids and racially motivated hate crimes, Lopez-Haugabook and Marrero decided to move in numbers together. They created a 16-week training program and encouraged members to join trail races. The group has become a catalyst for support, providing places to stay, food, and other help to those in need.

Trail running is more than just a sport for Sin Miedo. It’s a way to find peace, connect with nature, and build a community that supports each other. As Lopez-Haugabook puts it, “We’re going to show up”, and that’s exactly what they’re doing.
