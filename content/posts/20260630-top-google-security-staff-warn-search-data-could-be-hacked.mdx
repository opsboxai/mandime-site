Europe’s pro-competition proposals could see Google Search and Android systems opened up, but Google’s top security staff are sounding the alarm. Heather Adkins, Google’s vice president of security engineering, warns that these changes could lead to a significant increase in fraud and cybercrime. "Within a short period of time on Android, we’d see a significant increase in fraud in the EU," Adkins tells WIRED. "The fraudsters are creative and informed. Past implementation date, I would give it maybe weeks before we began to see an increase in fraud in Europe."

The European Commission’s plans aim to force Google to share anonymized search data with rivals and allow other AI services to have more access to the Android operating system. However, Google argues that the proposed anonymization techniques contain "deep weaknesses," and that the data can be reidentified. "Privacy engineers have proved that this data can be easily reidentified. If data can be reidentified, it is not anonymous in the first place," says David Lewis, Google’s director of privacy advisory for Europe, the Middle East, and Africa.

The stakes are high. Google’s search business makes up about 90 percent of the worldwide search market, and the company is concerned that sharing this data could expose users to privacy risks and cyber threats. While some competitors argue that the plans have less privacy and security impacts than Google suggests, the reality is that the data being shared is unique and sensitive. "Whether Google has a vested interest or not is irrelevant to the question of whether millions of people's most private questions may end up with someone they don't know and never expected would see their searches," Lewis adds.

As the European Commission prepares to make final decisions on these proposals, it’s clear that finding a balance between competition and privacy is crucial. The potential for increased fraud and data breaches is real, and the consequences could be severe. The Commission needs to ensure that any changes to Google’s systems are thoroughly vetted to protect users’ privacy and security.
