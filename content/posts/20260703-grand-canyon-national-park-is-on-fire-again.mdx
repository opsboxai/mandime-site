Almost exactly a year after the Dragon Bravo Fire decimated the Grand Canyon, lightning has struck again, igniting the Cliff Spring Fire. This time, it's burning through 171 acres of grass, brush, pinyon pine, and juniper trees below the North Rim. The fire is currently 0% contained, with firefighters using helicopters to drop water and limit the spread in steep, inaccessible terrain.

The timing couldn't be worse. The Grand Canyon is still reeling from the Dragon Bravo Fire, which burned 149,399 acres and destroyed over a dozen buildings, including the historic Grand Canyon Lodge. The North Rim was closed to visitors until late May 2026. Now, with another fire already underway, it's clear that the region is facing a relentless cycle of destruction.

The new U.S. Wildland Fire Service, launched in January, is facing its first true test this fire season. Experts predict an extremely active season due to drought and low snowpack. The stakes are high, and the pressure is on to prove that they can handle these fires more effectively than their predecessors. The debate over the NPS's response to the Dragon Bravo Fire is far from settled, and this new blaze will undoubtedly reignite that conversation.
