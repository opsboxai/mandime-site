There’s a reason why hotel sleep is the best sleep. The air-conditioner is set to sub-arctic temperatures, and the thick curtains keep the room cloaked in darkness. But at home, especially during the summer, it’s a different story. Your bedroom can feel stuffy, and you might find yourself tossing and turning, sweating through your sheets. Here’s how to sleep better in hot weather, according to sleep experts.

In a perfect world, your bedroom temperature would stay between 60℉ and 67℉ year-round. Dr. Ellen Stothard, a sleep and circadian scientist, explains that this temperature range is ideal because it aligns with the body’s natural cooling process, which signals the brain it's time to sleep. Research shows that sleeping in a too-hot environment can disrupt sleep continuity, increase wakefulness, and shorten critical REM and deep sleep stages.

If you can’t keep your room in the ideal range, there are still ways to cool down. Start with your bedding. Natural fibers like linen, cotton percale, and bamboo-derived fabrics are the best materials for summer bedding. Dr. Roger Washington, the medical director of the Sleep to Live Well Foundation, advises that these fabrics are breathable and less likely to trap heat. For cotton sheets, aim for a low thread count between 200 and 400, as a higher thread count can form an insulation barrier that keeps heat in.

Sunlight shining through your windows can make your room hotter. Dr. Scott Rosenberg, a sleep medicine doctor, recommends precooling your room a few hours before bed by lowering the blinds and curtains. If you have a fan, turn it on to circulate the air. Also, avoid charging your electronics in your room, as they generate heat. Charge your phone during the day so it’s fully charged by night.

Staying hydrated is crucial, especially in hot weather. Dr. Washington advises drinking about 85 ounces of water a day when it’s above 85℉. Dehydration raises your core temperature, making it harder to cool down. Eating a light evening meal can also help, as big meals raise body temperature. Dr. Rosenberg notes that spicy and carbohydrate-heavy meals can increase body temperature, so it’s best to avoid them at least two hours before bedtime.

Lastly, taking a warm shower before bed can actually help you sleep better. Dr. Washington explains that a warm shower causes blood vessels to dilate, which helps release heat from your body, making it easier to cool down and fall asleep.

So, the next time you’re sweating through the night, try these tips to get a better night’s sleep. Your body, and your sheets, will thank you.
