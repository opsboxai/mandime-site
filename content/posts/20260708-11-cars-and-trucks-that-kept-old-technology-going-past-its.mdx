When Jeep launched the first-gen Wagoneer in 1963, it was a game-changer. Luxurious and spacious, it quickly became a favorite among the wealthy. But here's the kicker: the Wagoneer stuck with old-school tech, like a carbureted 5.9-liter V8, right up until its demise in 1991. This V8, producing a modest 140 hp, was one of the last carbureted engines in a passenger vehicle in the U.S. The result? A fuel economy of just 11 mpg combined. 

Jeep wasn't alone in clinging to old tech. The Corvette C7, a sports car that you'd expect to be cutting-edge, used transverse leaf springs until 2019. These springs, while not as advanced as coil springs, were reliable and compact, giving the C7 a cargo capacity of 15 cu. ft. The setup worked well, especially in the lower-powered variants, but the Z06 and ZR1 models struggled with traction issues. The C8's mid-engine design and coil springs solved these problems, but the C7's use of leaf springs shows how sometimes old tech can still get the job done.

Toyota's Tacoma is another example. Until 2023, the Tacoma came with rear drum brakes, a feature that's practically ancient in today's automotive world. But these drum brakes were incredibly durable, with some owners reporting 200,000 miles of use. While disc brakes perform better in terms of heat management and overall braking performance, especially for towing, the Tacoma's drum brakes were a testament to reliability and longevity.

These examples show that sometimes, old tech isn't just about nostalgia. It can be about practicality, durability, and cost-effectiveness. So, the next time you see a car or truck with outdated features, remember that there's often a good reason for it.
