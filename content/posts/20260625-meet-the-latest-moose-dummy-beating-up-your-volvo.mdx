Volvo's latest crash test involves a massive 800-pound moose dummy, and it's a sight to behold. The orange Volvo plows straight into the dummy, resulting in a spectacular display of broken glass and crumpled metal. This isn't just a stunt; it's part of Volvo's decades-long commitment to improving safety features in the face of moose collisions.

Back in the 1980s, Volvo started with actual moose cadavers, which they described as "a water-filled sack" in head-on impacts. Over time, they moved to synthetic dummies, with the current version made of 116 rubber discs connected by steel parts. These tests have led to significant design changes, including improvements to the structure around the windshield, A-pillars, roof beams, and windshield retention. Safety belt pretensioners are also activated in large-animal collisions, further restraining occupants.

Lotta Jakobsson, Volvo's senior technical specialist for safety, notes that these advancements have enhanced occupant protection in severe large-animal impacts. In 2016, Volvo introduced Large Animal Detection, a feature that uses cameras and radar to identify large animals and automatically brakes to avoid collisions. This technology has since been adopted by other car brands, with the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration requiring all cars and light trucks in the U.S. to include automatic braking systems by 2029.

Several Volvo owners have praised the brand's safety features after surviving moose collisions. One Reddit user credited his Volvo's automatic braking system for reducing damage during a deer collision, while another shared an image of his busted windshield after hitting a moose, stating, "This vehicle saved my life today."

While automatic braking systems can prevent collisions with animals, they don't always prevent secondary collisions with vehicles behind you. This was evident when a Reddit user's mom hit the brakes to avoid deer, resulting in a three-car collision. Despite the damage, she was safe, highlighting the importance of these safety features.

Will widespread adoption of automatic braking systems make roads safer for both humans and moose? Only time will tell, but Volvo's commitment to safety is clear.
