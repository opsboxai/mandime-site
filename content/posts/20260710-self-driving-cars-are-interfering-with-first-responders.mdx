NHTSA administrator Jonathan Morris called reports of self-driving cars blocking emergency vehicles "unacceptable." And he's right, it's a serious issue that needs to be addressed pronto. Imagine this: you're in a critical situation, and an ambulance or fire truck is trying to get to you, but a self-driving car is just sitting there, blocking the way. That's not just frustrating; it's dangerous.

Waymo and Zoox have been in the spotlight for these incidents, with reports of their vehicles freezing in emergencies, blocking fire stations, and generally not responding to flashing lights or hand signals. The problem is that these vehicles were deployed too quickly, with too many on the road before they were fully ready. The tech is still in its infancy, and it's clear that developers need to step up their game.

The NHTSA is taking action, scheduling meetings with each company to discuss solutions and even threatening enforcement action if they don't address these safety concerns. This isn't just about making sure self-driving cars can navigate a busy intersection; it's about ensuring they can operate safely in all scenarios, including emergencies. The stakes are high, and the public's trust is on the line. Let's hope these companies can get their act together before someone gets hurt.
