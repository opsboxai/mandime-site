Hand-fishing for catfish used to be something you did alone in a river at dawn, not something you announced to 50,000 followers. But noodling—the practice of wading into murky water and literally sticking your arm into a catfish's mouth to wrestle it out—has exploded across the South, and social media is almost entirely responsible. Fred Dreier went down to see what the actual appeal was beyond the clip, and the answer is more honest than you'd expect: it's the tug that gets you, but the video is what keeps it alive.

The mechanics are simple and insane. You find a hole in the riverbank or under a log, shove your hand in, and wait for a catfish to bite. Once it does, you've got maybe thirty seconds to muscle a fish that could weigh 50, 60, even 80 pounds out of its hiding spot using nothing but grip and leverage. Your arm is the bait. Your body is the rod. There's no equipment, no skill cap, no certification required. In some states it was literally illegal until recently. That combination of danger, immediacy, and total accessibility makes it perfect for viral content. A guy gets chomped, drags out a massive fish, screams, and the algorithm does the rest.

What Dreier found, though, is that the guides doing this now aren't cynical about the social media angle. They know it's the gateway. But once someone's actually in that water and feels a 40-pound catfish clamp down on their forearm, the phone doesn't matter anymore. The tug is real. The adrenaline is real. The bruises are real. The Instagram post is just proof it happened. It's not the hardest part of noodling because it's the last part, and by then you've already committed to the absurdity of the whole thing.
