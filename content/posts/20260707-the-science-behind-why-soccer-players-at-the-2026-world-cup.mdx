Holes in socks have become a bizarre trend at the 2026 World Cup, with players cutting up their gear to find relief. But why are they doing it? The answer lies in a mix of biomechanics and personal comfort. 

Professional soccer socks are designed to be snug, providing support and stability. However, for some players, this tightness can cause discomfort, leading to a tingling and numb sensation in the calf area. During intense play, the calf muscles expand and contract, and the constant pressure from the sock can exacerbate this discomfort. Cutting holes in the socks is a way for players to "release tension" and feel more comfortable on the field.

While there's no scientific evidence to suggest that cutting holes in socks provides a competitive advantage, the practice has gained traction due to its perceived benefits. Sports medicine experts note that the phenomenon is more about personal comfort and confidence than any physiological advantage. In high-stakes sports, feeling unrestricted can boost a player's confidence, even if the actual performance remains unchanged.

So, the next time you see a player with holes in their socks, remember it's not just about fashion, it's about finding that extra bit of comfort and confidence on the pitch.
