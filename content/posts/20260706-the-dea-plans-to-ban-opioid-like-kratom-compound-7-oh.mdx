The DEA is about to ban 7-OH, a potent kratom compound that's been called "gas station heroin." This isn't just a regulatory move; it's a significant win for the mainstream kratom industry, which has been fighting to distance itself from the more dangerous synthetic versions of the plant.

7-OH, found in gummies, drinks, and capsules sold in gas stations and smoke shops, has been linked to severe health risks, including addiction and withdrawal symptoms. The DEA's decision to temporarily schedule it as a Schedule I drug, alongside substances like heroin, is a clear signal that the government sees it as a serious public health threat.

But here's the twist: this ban is a major victory for the kratom industry, which has been lobbying for stricter regulations on synthetic compounds like 7-OH. The industry has been working closely with government officials, including President Trump, to legitimize natural kratom, which has analgesic and antidepressant properties when taken in low doses. The DEA's move to ban 7-OH helps to clean up the market and protect the reputation of natural kratom products.

The ban is also a response to the growing concerns about synthetic opioids and the whack-a-mole nature of trying to control them. As one 7-OH user put it, "I don’t want to go back to that." But with the ban looming, there's a real concern about what people in her situation might turn to next. The stakes are high, and the DEA's decision could have far-reaching consequences for both the kratom industry and public health.
