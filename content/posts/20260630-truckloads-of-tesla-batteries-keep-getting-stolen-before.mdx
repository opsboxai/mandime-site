Nine major suspected cargo thefts happened at Tesla’s Nevada battery factory in January alone, according to sheriff’s records obtained by WIRED. That's right, truckloads of Tesla batteries are getting stolen before they even leave the factory. It's an epidemic, according to Storey County Sheriff’s Detective Sam Hatley, who has been investigating these cases.

The thefts aren't just a nuisance; they're costing millions. Trailers containing millions of dollars worth of Tesla car and home batteries have been stolen at least 11 times since last December. These aren't random acts of petty crime; they're strategic operations carried out by organized groups exploiting gaps in Tesla's security protocols. The thieves are using fake IDs and exploiting loose relationships with the truckers who transport Tesla's products.

Tesla has tightened its procedures, including verifying the identity of drivers at the factory gate, but the thefts are still happening, albeit less frequently. The broader issue is part of a larger trend: shipping thefts in the US roughly doubled from 2022 to 2024, costing companies nearly $18 million a day. That's a lot of money, and it's not just Tesla that's feeling the pinch.

What's particularly worrying is the sophistication of these operations. These aren't just opportunistic thieves; they're organized groups with a clear strategy. The fact that they can pull off these heists repeatedly shows just how vulnerable the supply chain can be. It's a reminder that even the world's most valuable automaker isn't immune to the risks of modern-day cargo theft.
