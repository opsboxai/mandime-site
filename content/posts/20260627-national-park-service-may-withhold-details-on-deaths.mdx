Four recent deaths in national parks have gone unreported by government officials, signaling a shift in how the National Park Service (NPS) handles public information on fatalities and serious injuries. The Interior Department, which oversees the NPS, has issued new guidelines that restrict park staff from directly notifying the public about these incidents.

GearJunkie writers have long reported on mishaps and accidents within national parks, where details of deaths and serious injuries were typically released within days of the incident. The goal was to educate the public and prevent future tragedies. But since President Trump took office, the Interior Department has taken control of how information is disseminated, and now, a December memo outlines a new policy that prohibits park staff from directly sharing details of deaths or serious injuries with the public.

The Interior Department claims the guidance is meant to create a more consistent approach to incident communications and is not intended to conceal fatalities or delay information. However, recent events suggest otherwise. While some deaths, like three heat-related deaths at Grand Canyon National Park and an NPS employee who died from falling into a crevasse on Denali, have been reported, four other deaths within the last week have gone unreported.

The Washington Post reported that a motorcyclist died in Great Smoky Mountains National Park and a body was found in the desert at Organ Pipe Cactus National Monument. Yet, neither the NPS nor the Interior Department has released any statements about these incidents.

Interior Department spokespersons have disputed that anything substantive has changed, stating that they continue to provide public safety information and statements as appropriate. However, former park superintendents like Dan Wenk, who served as head of operations for the park system and superintendent of Yellowstone National Park, have defended the past practice of quickly sharing information about park deaths.

"The basic process was always to get people as much information as you could give them as soon as possible around an incident," Wenk told The Post.

This shift in policy raises concerns about transparency and public safety. For outdoor enthusiasts and park visitors, knowing the details of these incidents can be crucial for understanding risks and taking necessary precautions. The new guidelines may hinder this vital information flow, potentially putting future visitors at risk.
