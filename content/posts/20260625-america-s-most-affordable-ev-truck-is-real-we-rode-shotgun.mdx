The Slate electric truck, priced at just $24,950, is set to redefine the EV market with its utilitarian design and impressive specs. We got a first-hand look at this back-to-basics vehicle, and it's a breath of fresh air in a world dominated by flashy tech and unnecessary features.

The Slate's most striking feature is its stripped-down interior. No touchscreen, just old-school knobs for the AC and a column shifter. The crank windows might seem like a step back in time, but they're a testament to durability and affordability. The truck's design philosophy is simple: if it doesn't need to be there, it's not there. This approach extends to the exterior, where a custom polypropylene-and-talc composite body ensures that dings and dents are seen as patina, not flaws.

During our ride-along, the Slate proved to be surprisingly zippy. With 135 kW and 264 Nm of torque, it accelerates from 0 to 60 in just 8 seconds, feeling even faster thanks to its compact size and rear-wheel drive. The interior, despite the truck's small footprint, feels spacious, with high sight lines and ample legroom designed for both tall and short drivers.

The recent update to the Slate's battery chemistry from NMC to LFP has boosted its range by 37 percent, now estimated at 205 miles. This change not only increased efficiency but also allowed for full charging without degrading the battery. Level 3 DC fast charging can get you from 20 to 80 percent in about 30 minutes, while Level 2 charging at home takes around four hours to fully charge the truck.

The accessory marketplace, launching alongside preorders, offers over 175 items, with many under $500. Partners like Yakima, Thule, and Camo ensure that the Slate can be customized to suit any adventure, from camping to mountain biking.

With first deliveries expected in Q4 2026, the Slate is not just a truck; it's a statement. In a market saturated with high-tech features, the Slate stands out for its practicality and durability. Whether you're a gearhead or just looking for a reliable, affordable EV, the Slate is worth a serious look.
