Christopher Nolan just made a three-hour movie about ancient Greece without a single digital effect, which tells you everything you need to know about why ranking his work matters. The Odyssey just hit theaters, and it's the kind of ambition-on-steroids project that only Nolan can pull off: practical ships, real stunts, actual scope. But here's the thing—his best film might not be the biggest one, and his biggest might not be the one that hits hardest.

The problem with ranking Nolan is that he doesn't make the same movie twice. Memento is a puzzle box that works backwards. The Dark Knight is a crime thriller that happens to have a guy in a cape. Inception is an heist film dressed up as sci-fi philosophy. Dunkirk is a wartime procedural told across three different timelines simultaneously. Each one demands something different from you as a viewer, and each one commits completely to that demand. He doesn't phone it in. He doesn't add crowd-pleasing filler. Every choice is in service of the movie he set out to make, which is why his worst films are still sharper than most people's best.

What separates the truly great Nolans from the very good ones usually comes down to heart. Interstellar works because it's fundamentally about a father trying to save his daughter, wrapped in wormholes and tesseracts. Oppenheimer works because it's about a man grappling with the consequences of his own brilliance. The ones that miss tend to be the ones where the concept starts to matter more than the character, where you're watching the machinery tick rather than feeling the stakes. His technical mastery is never in question. The question is always whether he's made you care about the people stuck inside all that brilliance.
