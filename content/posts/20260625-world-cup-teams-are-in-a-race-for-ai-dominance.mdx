The sheer scale of data being recorded at this summer’s World Cup is mind-boggling. FIFA will track around 150 million data points per match, with sensors inside the ball logging 500 movements per second. This data deluge is transforming the game, from player scouting to tactical decisions, and it's all powered by AI.

Patrick Lucey, chief scientist at Stats Perform, explains that soccer's complexity is akin to autonomous vehicles, with countless permutations and trajectories to analyze. Teams are leveraging this data to gain a competitive edge, from penalty analysis to lineup optimization. England, for instance, can now analyze every penalty taker for an opponent in just five hours, down from five days.

Smaller nations are also finding innovative ways to use technology. Curaçao, with a population of roughly 159,000, qualified for the World Cup by using data to track eligible players and plan scouting trips. But the real question is whether this data democratization can truly level the playing field.

FIFA has introduced a bespoke AI agent, Football AI Pro, to ensure every team has access to advanced analytics. This tool, resembling a ChatGPT-style interface, allows coaches to ask questions and analyze matches in 3D. However, the cost of AI tools and the expertise to operate them remains a significant barrier for smaller nations.

Jan Wendt, cofounder of PLAIER, an AI platform working with clubs and national teams, believes that partnering with established external companies could be a more efficient option for smaller nations. Yet, the challenge remains in distilling vast amounts of data into actionable insights.

The data gap between wealthier and poorer nations could still widen, potentially distorting competition. FIFA's efforts to provide technology to all teams are commendable, but whether it's enough to close the gulf remains to be seen. The World Cup is not just about the game on the pitch; it's also about the tech behind the scenes, and the stakes have never been higher.
