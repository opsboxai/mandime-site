Sotheby’s is auctioning off a 67-million-year-old T. rex skeleton named Gus, and it’s expected to fetch up to $30 million. But here’s the kicker: Gus could end up in a private collection, effectively disappearing from scientific research. 

This isn’t just a one-off issue. Private collectors are increasingly outbidding museums for fossils, and auction houses like Sotheby’s are fueling the trend by hyping up these sales. The result? Fossils that could provide invaluable insights into our past are being locked away in private collections, where they’re essentially lost to science. 

Take Gus, for instance. Sotheby’s is touting it as one of the most complete T. rex skeletons ever found, with 183 fossil bone elements. But the auction house is also spinning a narrative about bite marks on the bones, suggesting Gus might have battled or been scavenged by other T. rexes. The problem? This claim isn’t backed by solid evidence. In fact, paleontologists argue that these holes are more likely the result of infections, not battles. 

The bigger concern is that when fossils like Gus end up in private hands, they’re no longer accessible for scientific study. Museums simply can’t compete with the deep pockets of private collectors, leaving researchers in the dark. This trend isn’t just about losing a few specimens; it’s about losing the ability to understand our planet’s history. 

So, while Sotheby’s is hyping up the sale of Gus, the real story is about how wealth is upending science. The auction might be a spectacle, but it’s also a stark reminder of how money can sideline scientific progress.
