Three local anglers took Gear Junkie through Southern Florida's best-kept fishing secrets, and the conversation kept circling back to the same problem: the water's getting worse, and they know it.

These aren't casual weekend fishermen. They've spent years reading tide charts, tracking mullet runs, and knowing exactly where tarpon stack up in the backcountry. But lately they're seeing fewer fish, murkier water, and algae blooms that kill off entire areas for months. The honey holes still exist, but they're harder to find and shorter-lived than they used to be.

What made this worth reading wasn't just the spot recommendations. It was the underlying tension between loving a place enough to fish it hard and loving it enough to admit it needs help. These guys know the Everglades better than most scientists. They're watching it degrade in real time. And they're not pretending it's someone else's problem. That's the real story here: anglers becoming de facto watchdogs for a ecosystem most people only see from an airplane.
