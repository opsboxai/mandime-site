Sylvie Andrews and her partner lost everything when the Eaton Fire tore through Altadena, California, in January 2025. But while they were fighting to save their home, others were betting on the fire's outcome on prediction market platforms like Polymarket. People spent $1.2 million betting on how much damage the fires would cause, how long they would last, and whether they would spread to other areas.

For Andrews, this is "morally reprehensible." The idea that someone would profit from the destruction of her home and community is deeply unsettling. "The fact that someone would feel OK doing that flabbergasts me," she said. Susan Sherman, who lost her childhood home in the Palisades Fire, echoed this sentiment, calling the betting "crass and heartless."

The ethical concerns don't stop there. Prediction markets tied to wildfires could incentivize arson. Unlike natural disasters like hurricanes, fires can be started and manipulated quickly by a single person. This risk is so significant that the US Forest Service has stated that systems linking financial gain to wildfire outcomes are incompatible with their mission. "Imagine what a bad actor might do," said Ann Skeet, senior director of leadership ethics at Santa Clara University. "A market that might support that kind of activity is a dangerous market."

Moreover, betting on a wildfire's outcome diminishes the value placed on human life. "When you start gambling on somebody’s potential death or harm, you’re really diminishing the value that you’re placing on human life," Skeet added.

While platforms like Wyldfyre claim to provide a public good by crowdsourcing information, firefighting agencies aren’t buying it. The Forest Service and Cal Fire both stated they do not use prediction market data for wildfire forecasting. Instead, they rely on validated science and data from federal partners like the National Weather Service and the National Interagency Fire Center.

In the end, prediction markets for wildfires are a stark reminder of how financial gain can overshadow human suffering. For those who lost everything, the idea of betting on their tragedy is a painful reality that underscores the need for stricter regulations and ethical considerations.
