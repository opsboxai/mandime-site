Xinzhou Wu, head of automotive at Nvidia, is navigating a tech landscape where his company's GPUs are in hot demand for both AI and self-driving cars. But here's the kicker: even he, a top exec, has to fight for resources within Nvidia. That's right, the head of automotive is battling with his own company to get the compute power needed for autonomous driving systems.

This internal tug-of-war highlights just how intense the competition is for Nvidia's GPUs. On one side, you've got the AI boom, where every tech company is clamoring for those chips to power their latest AI models. On the other, you've got the auto industry, slowly but surely integrating these GPUs into their vehicles to push towards full autonomy.

Xinzhou's unique perspective, having worked in both Chinese OEMs and now at Nvidia, offers a deep dive into how the auto industry is evolving. He talks about the "software-defined vehicle," a term that's been thrown around a lot but essentially means cars are becoming more like computers on wheels. The transition from gas to electric and the shift away from dozens of independent electronic control units (ECUs) to a handful of powerful computers is well underway.

But the real goldmine in this conversation is Xinzhou's take on the Chinese car industry. He points out that Chinese manufacturers have a head start because they're building on EV architectures from the ground up, rather than trying to transition away from gas cars. This gives them a significant advantage in the race to autonomy.

And let's not forget the elephant in the room: Tesla. Xinzhou's assessment of Tesla's full self-driving capabilities without lidar is a must-listen. His answer might surprise you, especially if you're skeptical about Elon Musk's claims.

In a world where the auto industry feels like it's stuck in neutral, Xinzhou's insights provide a fresh perspective on the rapid changes happening under the hood.
