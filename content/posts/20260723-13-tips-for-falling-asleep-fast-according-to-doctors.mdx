You're lying awake at 11:47 PM knowing you have to be up at 6, doing math about how many hours of sleep you can still salvage. That spiral is the problem. Here's what actually works.

The big one most people miss: your bedroom temperature. Doctors agree on this more than almost anything else in sleep science. You need it cool, around 65 to 68 degrees Fahrenheit. Your body naturally drops its core temperature to fall asleep, so fighting that with a warm room is like trying to swim upstream. If adjusting the thermostat isn't an option, a fan does double duty—white noise plus air circulation.

Stop checking your phone 30 to 60 minutes before bed. This isn't new information, but the reason matters: blue light suppresses melatonin, your brain's sleep signal. More importantly, scrolling keeps your mind in active mode when it needs to shift down. Same applies to screens in general. The temptation to "just check one thing" has killed more sleep attempts than bad mattresses.

Caffeine hangs around longer than you think. A cup at 3 PM is still in your system at bedtime. Most doctors recommend cutting it off by 2 PM, or earlier if you're sensitive. Alcohol looks like it helps (you get drowsy), but it fragments sleep later in the night and leaves you wired and tired the next day. Not worth it.

Your schedule matters more than your mattress. Going to bed and waking up at the same time every day, even weekends, keeps your circadian rhythm locked in. Your body learns when sleep is supposed to happen and makes it easier. That means Saturday doesn't get a free pass to stay up until 2 AM. Consistency wins every time.
