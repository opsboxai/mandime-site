Imagine this: a single cyberattack cripples 5,000 water utilities across the US, leaving hospitals, data centers, and entire communities in the dark. This isn’t a sci-fi movie; it’s a scenario that played out in a recent war game, and it's chillingly plausible.

Joshua Corman, a former CISA strategist, led a group of insurance executives through a simulated cyberattack that started with a few burst water mains and quickly spiraled into a full-blown crisis. Hospitals were evacuated, food supply chains were disrupted, and cloud services went down. The kicker? The attack was allegedly carried out by China’s Volt Typhoon hackers, who have been pre-positioning themselves in US critical infrastructure for years.

The game's tension wasn't just in the scenario; it was also in the room, where participants were denied bathroom breaks to mimic real-world incident response. The stakes were high: deciding which clients to prioritize, whether to invoke an "act of war" exclusion, and ultimately, whether they would be seen as heroes or villains in the aftermath.

This isn't just a hypothetical. In 2023, Microsoft, the NSA, and CISA uncovered Volt Typhoon, a group of Chinese military-backed hackers who had infiltrated US critical infrastructure. They weren't just spying; they were laying the groundwork for potential sabotage, targeting everything from water utilities to oil pipelines. Brandon Wales, CISA’s former executive director, put it bluntly: "The only reason to target that sort of entity is to cause societal chaos in the United States."

The implications are stark. A single cyberattack could disrupt everything from healthcare to manufacturing, and the fallout could be catastrophic. The question isn't if it will happen, but when. And when it does, who will be left to pick up the pieces?
