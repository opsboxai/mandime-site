The ACLU just handed Massachusetts lawyers a new weapon: a detailed playbook for forcing cops to reveal what surveillance tech they're actually using to build cases against people. Dell Cameron reported on this toolkit, and it targets the real gap in the system—police departments buy facial recognition software, deploy AI to write reports, use cell-site simulators, and rarely disclose any of it to defendants or courts.

Here's why this matters. A guy can be arrested based on evidence gathered by a tool he has no way to challenge in court because he doesn't know it exists. The toolkit walks attorneys through Freedom of Information requests, interrogatory strategies, and expert testimony frameworks to pry those secrets open. It's not abstract privacy stuff. It's about whether the technology that identified you was accurate, whether it was tested, whether it had a known bias problem. A facial recognition system that fails 34 percent of the time on certain skin tones changes whether someone should be convicted. But if nobody knows it was used, nobody can argue about it.

The Massachusetts move is a direct response to years of police departments treating their surveillance arsenal like classified military hardware. They'll use license plate readers, DNA databases, predictive policing algorithms, and document it nowhere that a defense attorney can find. The ACLU toolkit essentially says: stop accepting that. Ask for the protocols. Ask for the error rates. Ask for the vendor contracts. Make them explain what they used and why it worked. It's not going to fix surveillance overnight, but it cracks the door open on the data and systems that were designed to stay hidden.
