Your weekly lifting routine might be doing more for your longevity than you think. According to recent research from Harvard, logging 90 to 120 minutes of strength training each week can significantly lower your risk of death from various causes. That’s just three or four half-hour workouts, or even two 45-minute sessions. 

The study, published in the British Journal of Sports Medicine, analyzed data from nearly 150,000 adults over 30 years. Compared to those who didn’t lift at all, those who hit the gym for 90 to 120 minutes a week saw a 13% lower risk of death from any cause, a 19% lower risk of cardiovascular death, and a 27% lower risk of death from neurological diseases. 

Strength training isn’t just about building muscle; it’s a powerful tool for reducing systemic inflammation, which is the root cause of most chronic diseases. Dr. Vonda Wright, an orthopedic surgeon and creator of Skeletal Longevity, explains that every time you contract a muscle against resistance, it releases signaling molecules called myokines that travel through your bloodstream and reduce inflammation. This leads to better blood sugar control, stronger bones, and improved metabolic health.

Moreover, resistance training helps protect against the gradual loss of strength and independence that comes with aging. Alex Rothstein, an exercise physiologist, notes that strength training allows you to interact with your environment more consistently and comfortably. It also remodels your vascular system, making arteries more elastic and compliant, which helps manage blood pressure and reduce heart attack risk.

Interestingly, the study found that anything more than 120 minutes of strength training didn’t provide additional longevity benefits. After about two hours a week, the perks plateau. So, if you’re already hitting the gym three times a week for a half-hour, you’re doing just fine. 

However, consistency and adherence over the long run are key. The study also found that combining strength training with aerobic exercise yielded the most benefit. Participants who did both saw a 45% lower risk of mortality than those who did neither. 

In summary, if you’re already lifting weights a few times a week, you’re on the right track. Just remember to stay consistent, and don’t forget to mix in some cardio for the best results.
