The Euclid space telescope has just dropped the most detailed visible-light image of the Milky Way's galactic bulge, capturing over 60 million stars in one fell swoop. That's right, 60 million stars, all in just 26 hours of observations. The image is a mosaic of nine separate exposures, each covering an area larger than the full moon. 

While the Hubble Space Telescope is known for its stunning images, Euclid's camera is a game-changer in terms of coverage and speed. Each of Euclid's pointings captures an area 270 times larger than Hubble's field of view. This means that while Hubble might take days to capture a similar image, Euclid can do it in a fraction of the time. 

The real kicker here is that this image isn't just a pretty picture. It's a treasure trove of data for scientists looking to study exoplanets through gravitational microlensing. The galactic bulge is a crowded region, making it ideal for detecting these events. With 51 known planetary systems already captured in this image, Euclid's data will serve as a reference archive for future missions, like the Nancy Grace Roman space telescope, enabling more precise measurements of exoplanet masses. 

In short, Euclid's image isn't just a stunning visual; it's a scientific goldmine that will help us understand the universe in ways we never could before.
