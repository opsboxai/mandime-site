Whether your goal is to pack on muscle, clock a new PR, or live past 100, understanding the difference between aerobic and anaerobic exercise is key. Luke Carlson, founder and CEO of Discover Strength, breaks it down: "Whether you’re training for aesthetic improvements, a specific activity or sport, or health-protective benefits, they're both effective." But as your goals narrow, knowing which type of exercise to prioritize becomes crucial.

The difference between aerobic and anaerobic exercise isn’t about what you do, but how you do it. Running can be aerobic or anaerobic, depending on the intensity. Aerobic exercise, like running, swimming, or rowing at a moderate intensity, relies on oxygen. Anaerobic exercise, on the other hand, involves short, high-effort bursts, like sprinting or lifting heavy weights, and is fueled by glycogen stored in the muscles. "With aerobic exercise, you’re working at a moderate intensity for longer periods of time, whereas anaerobic exercise is usually done at a high intensity for a much shorter duration," says Irvin Sulapas, MD, a sports medicine physician at Houston Methodist.

While aerobic and anaerobic exercise rely on different energy pathways, they often work together. A game of volleyball or tennis, for example, involves both aerobic and anaerobic elements. Similarly, a HIIT workout might tax your anaerobic system during intense efforts and your aerobic system for overall endurance and recovery between intervals.

Both types of exercise are essential for improving overall fitness. Aerobic exercise helps improve cardiovascular fitness, lung capacity, and endurance, while anaerobic exercise builds strength, power, and muscle mass. The benefits of these two kinds of fitness also feed into one another. "If you don’t have strong muscles, you're going to fatigue more quickly when you’re trying to do your aerobic exercises; similarly, you need aerobic fitness in order to up your intensity," Dr. Sulapas says.

For endurance athletes, aerobic training should make up the bulk of their routine. For strength-focused goals, anaerobic training should take precedence. "Choose to prioritize aerobic or anaerobic exercise based on your activity or sport," says Carlson. "Marathon running is 99.9 percent aerobic, so to prepare for it, you should do aerobic training. Football, meanwhile, is almost 100 percent anaerobic, so your training for that would be almost entirely anaerobic."

Longevity is another key factor. A 2022 study published in the British Journal of Sports Medicine found that both types of exercise can lower mortality risk, with the best results coming from a combination of the two. Both aerobic and anaerobic exercise support heart health, but each offers unique longevity benefits. "When you improve cardiovascular fitness, it also helps reduce your risk for diabetes and stroke, in addition to supporting cognitive health as you get older," says Dr. Sulapas. "Anaerobic fitness is important for longevity for a different reason. Keeping up with strength training helps preserve muscle mass as we get older, and it helps to maintain our strength and balance to reduce the risk of falls and fractures."

In the end, both aerobic and anaerobic exercise are crucial for overall health and fitness. Whether you’re training for a marathon, building muscle, or just trying to live a longer, healthier life, understanding the difference between these two types of exercise can help you tailor your workouts to your specific goals.
