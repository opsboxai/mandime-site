The four-minute mile used to mean something. Revered, impossible, the kind of thing Roger Bannister became immortal for in 1954. Now it's a benchmark that competent high school runners clear without much fanfare, and some of the faster kids are hitting it before they hit seventeen. That's not hyperbole dressed up as progress. That's the actual state of youth track right now.

What's happening is wild enough to have earned its own term: trackflation. Times that would have qualified for elite college programs a decade ago are now standard for serious high school competitors. The recruiting baseline keeps climbing. Middle schoolers are regularly running times that beat college runners from the 90s and 2000s. The jump isn't marginal. It's structural.

The causes are concrete and traceable. Better coaching infrastructure. Access to serious training regimens at younger ages. Track clubs that operate like semi-professional operations, complete with strength and conditioning programs, video analysis, and race strategy that used to be reserved for elite adults. Kids are also bigger and stronger than they used to be. Genetics matter, sure, but training age matters more now. A fourteen-year-old who's been doing structured workouts since middle school or earlier has years of adaptation that their predecessors simply didn't have. Add in better shoe technology, track surfaces that have improved measurably, and data-driven training plans that actually work, and the math becomes obvious.

The real consequence isn't just that times are faster. It's that the talent pool at the top is getting exponentially tighter. A sub-four-minute mile doesn't stand out anymore. Neither does a 15:30 5K in high school. The kids who will run in college are now the ones hitting times that would have been college-level records five years ago. For the average high school runner, that's demoralizing in a way that doesn't get talked about much. The goalposts moved and nobody asked permission.
