When Andrew Chan, MD, first started noticing young adults in their 20s and 30s showing up with colorectal cancer, he knew something was off. These weren’t your typical cancer patients, many were marathon runners, vegetarians, and even teetotalers. Yet, they were getting hit with a disease usually seen in folks over 65. 

Dr. Chan, a Harvard Medical School professor, isn’t alone in seeing this trend. The American Cancer Society reports that while overall colorectal cancer rates have dropped, they’ve spiked by 2.9 percent each year in people under 50. These cancers are also more aggressive, making them a ticking time bomb for younger generations.

The big question is: Why? Is it our diet, lifestyle, or something else entirely? Dr. Chan and his team are on the case with a $25 million project called Team Prospect, which aims to uncover the root causes. Early findings suggest that younger people with cancer are aging faster than their peers. Researchers found that those born after 1965 had a 23 percent higher likelihood of accelerated aging, linked to a higher risk of lung, gastrointestinal, and uterine cancers.

This isn’t just about cancer; it’s about how our bodies are aging faster than ever before. The science of aging is exploding, with breakthroughs like the epigenetic clock, which can estimate someone’s biological age based on DNA methylation. This means we might soon be able to get our biological age checked as part of a routine physical, identifying those who are aging too fast and monitoring them for diseases like cancer.

The takeaway? Our bodies might be aging faster, but there’s hope. Understanding and measuring biological age could be the key to catching cancer early and potentially slowing down the aging process. It’s a wild ride, but one that could save lives.
