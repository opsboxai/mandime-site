FIFA's new hydration breaks are stirring up a storm, but Mr. Moneybags over here is probably grinning from ear to ear. In the US, Mexico, and Canada, players will get a three-minute break around the 22nd and 67th minutes of each game. FIFA claims these breaks are about player welfare, but they're also creating predictable commercial breaks that networks like Fox are raking in millions from. 

Ghazi Saoud, a Chicago-based football fan, sees these breaks as "concealed advertisement breaks." He argues that football's charm lies in its continuity, and these scheduled interruptions disrupt that. David Goldblatt, a soccer historian, agrees, noting that nobody needs three minutes to drink water. Fox is estimated to make about $250 million from these breaks, according to BBC Sport. 

The broader commercialization of the World Cup is a contentious issue. Mark Dyreson, a professor at Penn State, sees this as a shift toward American-style sports entertainment. FIFA is trying to stay relevant in a crowded market, where leagues like the NBA and Formula 1 are competing for viewers. Simon Chadwick, a professor at SKEMA Business School, adds that FIFA is adapting to a changing media landscape and the warming climate. 

While FIFA defends the breaks as a safety measure, the commercial benefits are hard to ignore. The 2026 World Cup is expected to inject around $10.5 billion into the global advertising market. FIFA is clearly leveraging the US market to maximize revenue, with broadcast rights and sponsorships bringing in billions. 

For fans, it's a bittersweet reality. The World Cup is becoming a spectacle beyond the match itself, with halftime shows and sky-high ticket prices. But for Mr. Moneybags, it's all about the bottom line.
