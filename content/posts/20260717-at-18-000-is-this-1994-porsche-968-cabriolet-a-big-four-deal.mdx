There are maybe 2,000 Porsche 968 Cabriolets left on American roads. That scarcity alone makes the asking price on this '94 worth taking seriously, even at $18,000, because the 968 Cabriolet was never exactly common to begin with. Porsche only built them for five model years, and the open-top version was always the less popular sibling to the coupe. The result is a car that's genuinely hard to find now, which can either feel like owning something special or like owning something with no parts network and a dwindling pool of mechanics who understand it.

The 3.0-liter four-cylinder is honest work. It makes 236 horses and it came with a six-speed manual in its day, which is exactly the kind of simple, analog thing that's gotten scarce in the used market. Porsche didn't bolt turbochargers onto this engine or drown it in electronics. It's a car you can still understand. But that engine was also known for liking to crack its timing belt, and when it does, you're not taking it to a Toyota dealer. The 968 had a good reputation as the final iteration of a long lineage, but it wasn't bulletproof.

Eighteen grand for something this rare, with manual steering and a canvas roof that folds down, sits in that awkward space where it could be either a steal or a trap. The real question isn't whether the 968 Cabriolet is cool. It obviously is. The question is whether you're buying a forgotten piece of Porsche history or a beautiful way to learn an expensive lesson about what gets forgotten for a reason.
