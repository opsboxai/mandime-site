If you're in the market for a cheaper car, you might be willing to give up some of the modern luxuries and safety features that have become standard. According to Jalopnik readers, the list of features to sacrifice is long and varied. One reader suggests ditching all "safety" features like lane departure warnings and blind spot monitoring, arguing that if you're paying attention, you don't need them. Another points out that heated seats are unnecessary if you have fabric seats, and that the entire reason everyone loves them is because leather and fake leather suck in the cold.

The debate isn't just about making cars cheaper; it's about stripping away the unnecessary tech and features that have driven up costs. Many Jalops would happily revert to simpler times, with analog gauges, manual transmissions, and basic infotainment systems. One reader even suggests locking in features from 2014, a time when cars were less complex and more straightforward.

But the real kicker is the size. Modern cars have ballooned in dimensions, making them harder to park and maintain. A first-gen Outback was already spacious, but today's version is significantly larger, making it a challenge to fit in older garages. The trend towards bigger vehicles isn't just about comfort; it's about driving up costs and complexity.

So, if you're willing to give up the bells and whistles, you might find a cheaper car that's just as functional. But the real question is, are you ready to embrace a simpler, less tech-heavy driving experience?
