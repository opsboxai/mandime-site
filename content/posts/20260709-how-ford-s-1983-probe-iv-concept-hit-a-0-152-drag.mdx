The Ford Probe IV concept from 1983 hit a mind-blowing drag coefficient of 0.152, making it more slippery than most modern production cars. That's right, a car that's over 40 years old is still setting benchmarks in aerodynamics. The Probe IV wasn't just a pretty face; it was a marvel of engineering, with a body designed to cut through the air like a fighter jet. 

Ford's engineers spent two years refining the design, sending it through computer simulations and wind tunnel tests to get that ultra-low drag coefficient. The car's body was built in Italy, with a low-profile hood, raked windscreens, and flush glass to minimize drag. The sealed underbody and rear air inlets further smoothed out the airflow, while a rear-mounted cooling system and a 70-degree tilted engine helped keep the profile sleek.

The Probe IV's aerodynamic genius wasn't just about looks. It was 20% more fuel-efficient than a standard car and needed 60% less power to hit 50 mph. The car's air suspension and bespoke Goodyear tires were also ahead of their time, with features like speed-sensitive spoilers and run-flat technology that modern eco tires still haven't fully caught up to.

While the Probe IV never made it to production, its influence can be seen in the Ford Taurus, which debuted in 1986 with a rounded body that helped it achieve a drag coefficient of 0.32. The Probe IV may have been a concept, but it set the bar for what's possible in automotive aerodynamics.
