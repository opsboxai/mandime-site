The Think Family Database, a sprawling crime-prediction machine in Bristol, England, holds records on nearly half a million people, most of whom were unaware of its existence. Launched in 2016 by the Bristol City Council and Avon and Somerset Police, this database aggregates sensitive information like police intelligence reports, housing status, mental health records, and more. The goal was to create a “picture of threat, harm, and risk” through machine-learning models that assign risk scores to individuals.

This predictive analytics program includes at least 23 separate models designed to identify risks ranging from burglary to domestic abuse. One senior officer even described creating a “league table” of the area’s most dangerous criminals. However, the transparency and accuracy of these models have been questionable. At least two of these risk-scoring models were quietly abandoned after council staff deemed them unreliable. Government inspectors and independent reviewers have also highlighted a lack of transparency, warning that these systems could undermine public trust.

John Pegram, a local police accountability group leader in Bristol, filed a request to find out how his data was being used but was met with resistance. The police confirmed he was on the app but refused to provide further details. This lack of transparency and accountability is a growing concern, especially as the UK appears poised to embrace predictive analytics and AI across the criminal justice system.

The former chief constable of Avon and Somerset, Andy Marsh, now heads the national standard-setting body for forces across England and Wales. Marsh has been a vocal advocate for AI in policing, stating that it should be “injected like heroin” to speed up police work. However, the effectiveness and reliability of these tools remain questionable, with some models showing “genuinely poor predictive performance.”

The Think Family Database and its predictive models raise serious questions about privacy, accuracy, and public trust. As the UK moves forward with these technologies, it’s crucial to ensure that they are transparent, reliable, and accountable. The stakes are high, and the consequences of getting it wrong could be severe.
