In the 1960s, Joseph Weizenbaum created ELIZA, a chatbot that could mimic a therapist, and it quickly became a sensation. The program's ability to "fool" even its creator's secretary into thinking it was human set a precedent for the chatbots that followed. But ELIZA wasn't just a simple program, it was a complex piece of code that could generate a variety of personas, each with its own script.

The "DOCTOR" persona, in particular, became iconic. One of its earliest interactions, where ELIZA responds to a user with "Men are all alike," has been reprinted countless times. This exchange raises questions about the nature of human-computer interaction and the tendency to project human qualities onto machines. Weizenbaum himself was surprised by how quickly people formed emotional attachments to ELIZA, attributing empathy and understanding to a program that, in reality, had none.

This phenomenon, known as the "ELIZA effect," describes our tendency to see more intelligence and understanding in computer programs than they actually possess. Sociologist Sherry Turkle defines it as our inclination to project our own complexity onto machines, while cognitive scientist Douglas Hofstadter sees it as our susceptibility to reading more understanding into strings of symbols than warranted.

ELIZA's influence extends far beyond its initial creation. It laid the groundwork for the way we interact with AI today, from chatbots to generative AI systems. The program's ability to mimic human conversation and elicit emotional responses from users highlights the power of performative identity and the blurred lines between human and machine. As we continue to develop more advanced AI, understanding ELIZA's legacy is crucial for navigating the complex relationship between technology and human psychology.
