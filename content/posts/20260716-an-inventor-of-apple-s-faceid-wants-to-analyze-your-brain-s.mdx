One of the engineers who built Face ID is now trying to diagnose your brain the way your phone unlocks your face. Gidi Littwin's startup, Hemispheric, is training AI models on brain scans to spot depression, PTSD, and Parkinson's before a person even knows something's wrong. The idea sounds like science fiction, but the problem it's solving is real and urgent: most brain disorders get diagnosed by asking someone how they feel, not by looking at what's actually happening inside their skull.

Brain imaging exists, obviously. MRI machines have been around for decades. But they're expensive, slow, and most insurance won't cover them for mental health screening. A scan costs thousands and takes weeks to interpret. Littwin's bet is that AI can compress that workflow down to something as routine and accessible as a blood test. If he pulls it off, a doctor could catch depression or early Parkinson's during a standard checkup instead of waiting for a patient to hit crisis mode.

The hard part isn't the technology anymore. It's the data. AI models trained on brain scans need millions of images to learn patterns, and most of that data is locked in hospital systems or proprietary databases. Hemispheric has to build partnerships and convince clinics to share their scans, anonymized, so the models get smarter. It's the same gatekeeping problem that slowed down medical AI for years. But if they crack it, they're not just building another tool for hospitals. They're potentially rewriting how we catch and treat some of the hardest diagnoses in medicine. The guy who figured out how to make your phone recognize your face is now working on making your brain's problems visible before symptoms arrive.
