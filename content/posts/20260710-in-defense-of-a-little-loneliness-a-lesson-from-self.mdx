On the longest solo trip of my life, I stepped off a two-seat float plane onto the rocky shore of Upper Twin Lake in Alaska’s Lake Clark National Park. I was there to follow in the footsteps of Dick Proenneke, the self-taught naturalist whose 30-year solo life in the wilderness was captured in the PBS documentary "Alone in the Wilderness." Proenneke never married, never had children, and spent nearly three decades completely alone, save for the birds he fed by hand and the occasional bear that clawed at his logs.

“He must have been lonely out here,” a fellow traveler said during the park ranger’s tour of the cabin. On that chilly June morning, I found myself wondering the same thing. But I came to a different conclusion. Proenneke enjoyed solitude but not loneliness. Solitude feels intentional and rewarding, while loneliness causes anxiety and depression. He wasn’t a misanthrope; he welcomed visitors and even whittled walking sticks to match their height.

My aversions to crowds and clubs have always been a source of personal confusion. I’m not a misanthrope, but I’ve never been into team sports or double dates. As a reporter, I crave deeply personal interactions, but I’ve also found solace in solitude. After my marriage crumbled, I spent dozens of nights sleeping in tents, often in single-digit temperatures. I punished myself with trail runs and difficult hikes, hoping exhaustion would tamp down the urge to beg for answers. The mountains and the silence became my refuge.

In Alaska, I realized that solitude isn’t a sign of weakness; it’s a choice. It’s about finding a “vast inner solitude” as Rainer Marie Rilke advised. Dick Proenneke’s life in the wilderness wasn’t about loneliness; it was about embracing the peace and clarity that comes with being alone. In a world that often pushes us to seek constant social interaction, Proenneke’s example reminds us that there’s value in solitude. It’s a way to live, and sometimes, it’s the only way to heal.
