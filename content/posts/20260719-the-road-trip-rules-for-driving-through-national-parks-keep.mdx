Wildlife doesn't care about your insurance deductible. That's the core reality behind the National Park Service's strict driving rules, and it's something most road trippers discover too late, usually while white-knuckling the steering wheel as a bison casually blocks the entire lane.

National park roads operate under different physics than your commute. Speed limits are lower, not to annoy you, but because animals can step into traffic without warning, and at 45 miles per hour you actually have time to react. The bigger problem is that most visitors treat park roads like scenic byways where the real action is the view, not the constant calculation of what's around the next bend. A distracted driver doing 35 in a 25 zone is still a hazard. The rule exists because a collision with a 2,000-pound animal at any speed converts your car into a crumple zone and the wildlife into a tragedy that could have been prevented.

Distance perception fails in open landscapes. That elk across the meadow looks far away. It isn't. They move faster than you'd expect and they're curious about vehicles in ways that quickly turn dangerous. The NPS wants you staying in your car, staying on designated roads, and maintaining realistic speed expectations because the alternative is either dead animals or injured people, sometimes both.

The unsexy truth is that following the rules isn't oppressive. It's the difference between a memory and an insurance claim. Drive like the animals own the place, because in every meaningful sense, they do.
