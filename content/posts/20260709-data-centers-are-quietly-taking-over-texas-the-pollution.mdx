Omaira Garcia didn’t know her life was about to change until clouds of dust started engulfing her home in Abilene, Texas. The culprit? OpenAI’s Stargate data center, which began construction right next door without much public notice. 

This isn’t just a local issue; it’s a statewide trend. With over 300 data centers already in operation and 200 more in development, Texas is on track to become the nation’s leading data center market by 2030. The problem? A regulatory loophole allows these centers to build massive power sources with little oversight, thanks to minor air permits typically used for dry cleaners and autobody shops. 

The environmental impact is staggering. These data centers are locking in fossil fuel use for the foreseeable future, with some plants capable of emitting more greenhouse gases than entire countries. The Stargate facility alone could emit over 1.6 million tons of greenhouse gases annually. 

While proponents argue that these centers bring economic benefits, like new fire trucks and school expansions, the human cost is real. Residents like Garcia are left feeling blindsided and trapped. The irony is that while Texas is embracing the AI boom, it’s doing so at the expense of its residents and the environment. 

This isn’t just about data centers; it’s about the balance between progress and responsibility. As the AI industry booms, we need to ensure that the environmental and social costs are not overlooked. The stakes are high, and the consequences could be catastrophic.
