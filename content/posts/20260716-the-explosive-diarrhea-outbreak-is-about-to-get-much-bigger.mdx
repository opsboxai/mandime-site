The cyclosporiasis outbreak hitting the US right now is probably way worse than the official numbers suggest. Health officials are tracking cases tied to contaminated produce, but those confirmed cases are just the tip. Most people who get infected either don't seek medical care, don't get tested, or don't report it, which means the actual infection count could be multiples of what the CDC is announcing. That gap between confirmed cases and real ones matters because it means the outbreak could be spreading faster and wider than anyone's publicly admitting.

Here's what makes this worse: cyclosporiasis has a lag built in. Symptoms show up five to fourteen days after exposure, which means the people getting sick right now are eating contaminated food from weeks ago. The produce that's actively circulating in stores and distribution centers right now will start hitting people in the coming weeks. If the source hasn't been totally shut down and removed from the supply chain, case counts are going to climb hard. And because the parasite spreads through water and contaminated food rather than person-to-person contact, it's not something people can just avoid by staying home.

The real issue is that cyclosporiasis is a pain to diagnose. It causes explosive diarrhea, sure, but so do dozens of other things. A lot of people assume it's food poisoning and wait it out without calling a doctor. Those cases never make it into the official count, which means public health officials are flying blind on the actual scope. When you're trying to track a moving target and you can only see maybe ten or twenty percent of it, your ability to contain it drops fast. The numbers will probably look dramatically different in a month.
