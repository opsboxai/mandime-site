A June 13 hydrothermal explosion in Yellowstone’s Biscuit Basin left a 21-foot pool of boiling water and a 61-foot-long fissure with water reaching 194 degrees Fahrenheit. This event, while smaller than the July 2024 blast, still reshaped the area, creating new vents and a near-boiling fissure.

The explosion occurred at 5:09 a.m. MDT, with monitoring equipment detecting unusual seismic energy and low-frequency acoustic signals. A research camera captured a dark steam plume rising from the area, revealing newly opened vents and cracked ground. The event was a hydrothermal explosion, where hot water beneath the surface turns to steam, forcing water, mud, rock, and debris upward.

When geologists inspected the site on June 14, they found three groups of vents north of Black Diamond Pool. Hydrothermal water had flowed into the Firehole River, turning it milky gray downstream. The most significant change came a few days later, with a 21-foot by 17-foot pool of boiling water forming near the middle vent group. This pool later became active, spouting boiling water up to 30 feet into the air.

The new pool and vents offer scientists a unique opportunity to study hydrothermal explosions. The event happened close to a monitoring station installed in 2025, potentially providing valuable data to help predict future blasts. Biscuit Basin remains closed, with park officials cautioning visitors to stay on boardwalks and away from closed thermal areas.

For guys who love the outdoors, this event highlights the unpredictable and awe-inspiring nature of Yellowstone. The park’s thermal features can shift rapidly, making it a fascinating but dangerous place. Stay on the trails, and always respect the natural forces at play.
