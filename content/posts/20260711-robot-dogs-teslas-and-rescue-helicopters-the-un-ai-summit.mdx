Amid live coding sessions and Silicon Valley optimism, the UN’s AI for Good summit wrestled with a pressing question: Can global governance catch up before AI races beyond its control? This year's summit, now in its 10th year, was a whirlwind of tech demos, networking zones, and heated discussions about the future of AI.

The event, organized by the United Nations’ International Telecommunication Union (ITU), aimed to explore how AI could solve humanity's most pressing problems, from hunger to disease to climate change. But the summit was also a stark reminder of the tech industry's growing pains. While Silicon Valley execs and AI lab leaders were testifying to lawmakers about the risks of superintelligence, the UN was focused on more idealistic goals. 

The summit's idealism clashed with harsh realities. Speakers like Giulio Coppi, a senior humanitarian officer at Access Now, called out the humanitarian and public sectors for their overreliance on big tech. "We should be out of the age of innocence," Coppi said, demanding that organizations stop treating tech companies as their best friends. He pointed to a decade of opaque, multimillion-dollar deals funded by public money, where "you can’t even explain what's inside your tech stack, because it has kept changing."

The summit also highlighted the widening digital divide. Speakers argued that compute is no longer just a technology problem but a development problem. Syed Munir Khasru, chairman of the Institute for Policy, Advocacy, and Governance, emphasized that if AI is to serve communities beyond the richest markets, smaller, local LLMs running on cheaper hardware are essential.

Ultimately, the summit was a mix of lofty ideals and harsh realities. While there was plenty of talk, concrete action was harder to come by. The formation of a 44-member commission to shepherd in AI for Good, cochaired by Rwandan president Paul Kagame and Salesforce CEO Marc Benioff, was a step in the right direction. But as Bogdan-Martin noted, "No single stakeholder can shape the future of AI alone. It needs builders. It needs you."
