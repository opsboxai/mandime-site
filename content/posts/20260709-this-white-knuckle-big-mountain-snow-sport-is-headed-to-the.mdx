Imagine this: you’re careening down a jagged, snow-covered mountain, navigating through treacherous terrain with nothing but your wits and your board. That’s freeride skiing and snowboarding, and it’s about to hit the biggest stage of all, the 2030 Olympic Games.

Freeride skiing and snowboarding has been a cult favorite since the '90s, with its roots in the Xtreme Verbier contest in Switzerland. It’s a sport that’s all about choosing your own line down the mountain, with each descent judged on style, technical skill, and the sheer ballsiness of the run. Unlike the controlled, repetitive courses of traditional Olympic winter sports, freeride is about the raw, unpredictable thrill of natural terrain.

The International Ski and Snowboard Federation (FIS) acquired the Freeride World Tour in 2022, paving the way for freeride’s Olympic debut. This move reflects the sport’s massive growth from a niche scene to a global phenomenon, with fans and athletes spread across the world. The inclusion of freeride in the Olympics is a huge win for the sport, giving athletes a chance to represent their countries on the world’s biggest stage.

And it’s not just about the athletes. The 2030 Winter Olympic Games will also be the first to achieve gender parity, with roughly equal numbers of male and female competitors. This means 44 freeride athletes, 22 women and 22 men, will have the chance to compete for Olympic glory.

For Mia Jones, the 2026 FIS Freeride World Champion, the Olympic inclusion is a dream come true. “The energy surrounding the Olympics is truly unlike anything else,” she says. “I can’t wait to see it all go down!”

For guys who love the rush of freeride, this is a big deal. It’s not just about watching the best of the best compete; it’s about seeing a sport that’s all about individuality and raw talent get the recognition it deserves. So, mark your calendars for 2030. The freeride dream is about to become a reality.
