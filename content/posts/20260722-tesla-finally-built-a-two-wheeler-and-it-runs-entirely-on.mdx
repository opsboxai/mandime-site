Tesla just released a balance bike for toddlers and it sold out before most people knew it existed. The thing is made from magnesium, which is the same material aerospace engineers use to keep planes light enough to actually fly. For a two-year-old learning to shuffle forward on foot power alone.

The move is pure Tesla marketing. They're not making money on a kids' bike. They're selling the idea that even a balance toy has to be engineered down to the molecular level, that everything from a car platform to a toddler's first wheels deserves obsessive attention to materials. It works because it's partially true. Magnesium is genuinely lighter than aluminum. A lighter frame means a kid doesn't fight the bike just to move it, which actually matters when you're two feet tall and haven't figured out how limbs work yet.

What's wild is that it sold out. Not because balance bikes are some hot commodity. Because Tesla put "Tesla" on it, and because in 2024, that's enough. The company turned a toddler's training device into a flex, and the parents who care about this stuff couldn't click fast enough. The bike costs real money for what is essentially a fancy scooter without pedals. But if your kid's going to learn to ride anyway, you might as well tell people it's built from spacecraft materials.
