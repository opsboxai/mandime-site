The U.S. Army just sent its soldiers an email telling them to chill on the AI. They're running out of tokens.

Here's what actually happened: troops got word they were burning through their allocated compute credits so fast that the Army had to hit the brakes and tell everyone to dial back usage or face service cuts. It's the digital equivalent of your family group chat realizing you're about to blow through the monthly data plan before the billing cycle ends.

This matters because it's a concrete window into how aggressively the military is already leaning on AI tools in actual operations, not just in press releases or five-year plans. The fact that they're running dry this quickly says either the demand is massive, the token allocation was way too optimistic, or both. The Army clearly expected soldiers would use these systems conservatively. They didn't. That's telling. It suggests the tools are actually useful enough that people are reaching for them constantly, not abandoning them after a pilot phase like dead software usually does.

The bigger picture: the military has been sprinting to integrate AI everywhere, from logistics to targeting to decision support. But like any bureaucracy, they budgeted for what they thought they'd need and got blindsided by reality. Now they're in that awkward spot where adoption is working too well, the infrastructure can't keep pace, and everyone has to ration access like it's 2005. It's a good problem to have, technically. It's also the kind of constraint that kills momentum. Fix it or watch soldiers stop using the tools and drift back to old workflows.
