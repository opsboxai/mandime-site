A few hours after checking into a hotel in New York City, David Streever got a call from the front desk saying someone was looking for him. Little did he know, ICE agents had already paid a visit to his home in Rochester, dropping off a WARNING NOTICE that his online criticism of ICE might be a violation of federal law. This isn't an isolated incident. DHS has been going after people who criticize immigration policies, accusing them of doxing or threatening federal personnel. Over 100 investigations have been opened into "incidents of doxing and threats" involving ICE, with agents showing up to people's homes and polling places to deliver these ominous notices.

The stakes here are high. This isn't just about a few angry emails or social media posts. It's about the government using its power to intimidate and silence critics. The First Amendment isn't just about protecting you from arrest; it also guards against government retaliation or coercion. DHS's actions are a clear attempt to chill dissenting speech, and it's a slippery slope. If the government can show up to your door or hotel room over a strongly worded email, what's next? 

Adam Steinbaugh, a senior attorney with the Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression, puts it bluntly: "There is a broader pattern of the federal government using dubious legal resources or tools to deter speech." This isn't just about ICE; it's about the erosion of free speech and the chilling effect it has on public discourse. The government's heavy-handed approach is a stark reminder of how easily our rights can be undermined.
