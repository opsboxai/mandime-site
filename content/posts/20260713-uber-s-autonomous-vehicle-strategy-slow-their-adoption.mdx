Uber's strategy to slow down the adoption of autonomous vehicles is a bold move that could give them a significant edge over competitors. In New Jersey, Uber lobbyists are pushing for legislation that would require any platform offering driverless ride-hailing services to have human drivers serve 85 percent of its rides for three years. This move would effectively force self-driving vehicle developers like Waymo, Zoox, and Tesla onto Uber's app, limiting competition and solidifying Uber's position as the go-to platform.

Uber CEO Dara Khosrowshahi has made it clear that the company sees itself as the commercial platform for all autonomous vehicle players, not just a developer of its own self-driving tech. By pushing for "hybrid networks" where human drivers and robots work side by side, Uber is positioning itself as the essential middleman in the transition to autonomous vehicles. This strategy isn't just about protecting Uber's business model; it's about ensuring that the company remains the dominant player in the ride-hailing market, regardless of who's behind the wheel.

Uber's stance is also about addressing labor concerns. The company argues that its proposals are a compromise that could help get autonomous vehicle regulations passed, especially in the face of labor union opposition. However, critics see this as a way to stifle competition and create monopolies. Waymo, one of Uber's key partners, has already expressed concerns about limiting AVs to specific types of networks.

As the battle over autonomous vehicle regulations heats up in states like New Jersey and Washington, DC, it's clear that Uber is playing a long game. Whether this strategy will pay off remains to be seen, but one thing is certain: Uber isn't going down without a fight.
