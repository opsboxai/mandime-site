In the summer of 2024, former Army National Guard member Andrew Scott Hastings spent a sweaty afternoon packing boxes with parts he made using his 3D printer. These weren’t just any parts; they were homemade firearm lower receivers and "switches" capable of converting semiautomatic guns into fully automatic weapons. Federal prosecutors allege Hastings shipped these to al-Qaida operatives. This isn’t a one-off incident. In Colorado Springs, ATF agents busted two men for churning out hundreds of illegal machine gun conversion devices, hiding them in Lego boxes and shipping them across the country.

The killing of UnitedHealthcare CEO Brian Thompson in December 2024 by a partially 3D-printed Glock-style frame and a 3D-printed silencer brought 3D-printed guns, or "ghost guns," back into the public eye. These untraceable weapons have been around since 2013, when Cody Wilson created the first functional printed firearm. Despite efforts to regulate the files and websites hosting them, gun control advocates have faced legal hurdles, with courts often treating gun code as protected speech.

Now, California and New York are taking a new approach by regulating the printers themselves. Both states are pushing legislation that would require 3D printers to employ "print blocker" software to detect and prevent the printing of gun files. California’s AB 2047 and New York’s recent budget include provisions mandating that printers sold in these states come equipped with firearm-blocking technology. The specifics of this technology are still up in the air, leaving critics concerned about potential overreach and broad surveillance.

While these measures aim to curb the production of ghost guns, they raise questions about the balance between gun control and technological freedom. The flexibility in the legislation gives the printing industry room to experiment, but it also opens the door to broader surveillance. The stakes are high, and the outcome could set a precedent for how technology and gun control intersect in the future.
