If you've ever hit a plateau with your bench press, you're not alone. The bench press is the chest day staple, but if you're stuck at the same weight week after week, it's time to shake things up. According to top trainers and coaches, improving your bench press isn't just about lifting more; it's about lifting smarter.

First off, you need to practice more than just once a week. Luke Carlson, founder of Discover Strength, emphasizes that to get better at bench pressing, you have to practice the skill of bench pressing. That means dedicating at least two to three sessions per week to it. Mathew Welch, an exercise physiologist, recommends a three-day training split: a maximal strength day, accessory work, and a dynamic effort day. The dynamic effort day, where you move lighter weights as fast as possible, can be a game-changer for breaking through plateaus.

Proper form isn't just about injury prevention; it's about efficiency. Jesse Shaw, a sports medicine physician, points out that your feet play a crucial role in the bench press. Setting your feet flat on the ground and driving into the ground with your legs can make a significant difference in the amount of weight you can lift. This connection between your upper and lower body is key to maximizing your strength.

Sticking points, those areas where you consistently struggle to push through, can be addressed with targeted exercises. If you're having trouble locking out, focus on your triceps. Eddie Baruta suggests using chains to increase resistance as you push through the top of the motion. If you're getting stuck near the bottom, it might be a strength imbalance between your chest and back. Dr. Shaw recommends training the eccentric phase, or the lowering part of the rep, with heavier weights to spur strength gains.

In short, improving your bench press isn't just about lifting more weight; it's about refining your technique, increasing frequency, and addressing your weak points. So, if you're ready to smash through those plateaus, it's time to get strategic.
