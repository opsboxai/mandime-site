Flock's camera network has quietly become something way larger than a license plate reader. Law enforcement can now search for vehicles and people by visual characteristics—clothing color, body type, gait, whether someone's carrying something—using the FreeForm search tool. This isn't parsing metadata. This is visual surveillance at scale, running against footage from thousands of cameras across the country, many of them on private property without explicit consent from property owners.

The pitch was always simple: catch criminals faster. Flock cameras, mounted on poles and buildings, grab license plates and help departments solve crimes. Straightforward tech, reasonable use case. But the company's own materials show the system can now identify a person by how they walk, what they're wearing, what they're holding. That's a massive capability jump that most people installing these cameras probably didn't realize they were funding. A cop looking for someone involved in a robbery can now search "male, dark hoodie, backpack" across an entire city's camera network and watch everyone matching that description.

The problem isn't that the tool exists. The problem is the creep. Flock started narrow and has expanded its search capabilities without the kind of public debate or policy framework you'd expect for something this invasive. Property owners volunteer their cameras. Police departments adopt the system. And somewhere in that process, what was sold as a plate-reading tool became a people-tracking system. The ACLU and others have flagged this before, but the integration keeps happening, city by city, often without much local scrutiny. This is how surveillance expands in America now: incrementally, through private partnerships, under the radar of serious regulation.
