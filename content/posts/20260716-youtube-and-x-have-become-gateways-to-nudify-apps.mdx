The pipeline is real and it's cheap. A new study tracking how nudify apps spread found that YouTube and X aren't just hosting content about these tools—they're actively funneling users toward them. The math is grim: nonconsensual deepfake porn can be generated for a dollar per image, and the major platforms are essentially serving as the marketing department.

Here's how it works. Someone uploads a tutorial video or a post hyping the technology, complete with links and referral codes. YouTube's algorithm sees engagement and pushes it. X's algorithm does the same. Most people watching probably aren't serious about it—they're curious, they're joking, they're scrolling. But enough click through to make the economics work for the apps on the other end. One dollar. That's the barrier to entry for creating sexually explicit images of someone without consent. That's your actual floor.

The platform defense is predictable: we don't allow nonconsensual deepfake porn on our sites. True, technically. But that misses the actual problem. They don't have to host the final product to be part of the supply chain. Hosting the how-to content, the enthusiasm, the link—that's enough. They're the distribution network for radicalization into image-based sexual abuse. And because the moderation burden is theoretically on the platforms where the images land, not where the interest gets cultivated, nobody claims responsibility.

What makes this study matter is the mechanism it maps. It's not an accident or a loophole. It's a business model that works across three layers: awareness on mainstream platforms, conversion through affiliate links, and execution on sites designed to exist outside most regulatory pressure. YouTube and X didn't invent nudify apps. But they built the on-ramp.
