Scientists working on the side hustle? That's right. A team from the Technical University of Denmark used their spare time and leftover project funds to show how quantum computing can supercharge drug discovery. And they did it with a printer-sized quantum computer from ORCA Computing, which helped speed up their AI models.

The researchers generated novel peptides, short chains of amino acids that can bind to specific proteins in the body. This is a crucial step in developing vaccines and immunotherapies. The kicker? They found that their quantum-enhanced model produced more successful peptides, especially in cases where training data was scarce. This could be a game-changer for developing drugs for underserved populations and rare diseases.

The team's leader, DTU professor Timothy Patrick Jenkins, was initially skeptical about quantum computing. But after seeing the potential, he and his crew dove in, working weekends to prove that their predictions could connect to the real world. The results were promising, but they're not stopping there. They're now looking to apply this workflow to more advanced models and larger proteins, with the ultimate goal of making a significant impact in drug development.

This side hustle isn't just about proving a point; it's about making a difference where it's needed most. And with the potential to enhance synthetic antidotes for snakebite venom, it's clear that these scientists are onto something big.
