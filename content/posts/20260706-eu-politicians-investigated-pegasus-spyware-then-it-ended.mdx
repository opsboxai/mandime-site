Stelios Kouloglou, a Greek politician and member of the European Parliament’s PEGA Committee, was investigating the use of Pegasus spyware when his own iPhone was hacked with the same software. This isn’t just a case of irony; it’s a stark reminder of how vulnerable even those tasked with investigating these threats can be.

Kouloglou, who served as an MEP from 2015 to 2024, was shocked to learn that his device had been compromised multiple times. The irony is thick: he was investigating Pegasus while being targeted by it. “It was something really too reckless,” Kouloglou tells WIRED. The attacks, documented by Citizen Lab, raise serious questions about the security of parliamentary work and the integrity of the European Parliament itself.

Citizen Lab’s findings suggest that whoever was behind these attacks could have accessed internal information about the committee’s activities and findings, potentially violating EU parliamentary confidentiality requirements. The attacks occurred while Kouloglou was in the hospital and during key hearings, highlighting the brazen nature of the operation. “It’s open spyware season on Europe’s lawmakers,” says John Scott-Railton, a Citizen Lab senior researcher.

The implications are clear: spyware isn’t just a tool for targeting journalists and activists; it’s now being used to undermine the very institutions meant to protect against it. This isn’t just a breach of privacy; it’s a direct attack on the rule of law. As MEP Saskia Bricmont puts it, “The use of spyware not only violates the fundamental rights of the individuals concerned, but in this case also threatens the security and integrity of parliamentary work and of the European Parliament as a whole.”

The European Parliament has a “spyware screening system” available to all MEPs, but the recent findings suggest that more needs to be done to protect lawmakers from these threats. The attacks on Kouloglou and others highlight the urgent need for robust public-private collaboration and policy efforts to address the misuse of spyware. The stakes are high, and the absurdity of the situation is clear: those investigating spyware are being spied on themselves.
